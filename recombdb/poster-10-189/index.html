189. Constrained Gene Structure Annotation Using Exdomino


Michael Sparks, 1 Yuanbin Ru, 2 and Volker Brendel 3



Keywords: Bayesian models, Interpolated Markov Models, exon prediction

Abstract

Large-scale EST sequencing projects are providing vast collections of mRNA fragments that can be used to produce highly accurate gene structure annotations by spliced alignment against genomic sequences. This approach is limited, however, because for many genes - and -EST evidence does not cover their central portions (see Fig.1). While EST projects have been complemented by full-length cDNA sequencing projects, e.g., [3], this approach is expensive, time-consuming, and potentially unnecessary. Thus, there is demand for capable software to predict full-length transcripts based on partial EST coverage of genomic DNA.



We present a novel gene annotation tool, Exdomino, that incorporates Bayesian models [5] for splice site prediction, Interpolated Markov models [2] for exon recognition, and phase-constrained exon assembly, e.g., [1], to fill out partial spliced alignment-confirmed gene structure predictions generated by the GeneSeqer [4] program for the prediction of full-length gene structure. Exdomino offers advantages over traditional ab initio gene prediction methods such as the GenScan HMM [1]: first, by incorporating EST spliced alignment data, Exdomino is able to predict a complete transcript, rather than coding exons only (see Fig.1); and secondly, overall prediction accuracy is improved compared to ab initio-only programs.



We will also discuss applications of this program in which we refine current genome annotation of Arabidopsis thaliana and Oryza sativa gene structures.

Figure 1: Example illustrating the complete annotation of the Arabidopsis thaliana gene At5g02810 from chromosome five, encoding an ABI3-interacting protein. Shown are the following:
  1. Genbank's hypothetical gene annotation. The lighter triangle indicates the translation start codon, while the darker triangle indicates the stop codon.
  2. The complete exon structure as predicted by Exdomino. Notice that Exdomino incorporates the extended sequence of the At5g02810 transcript--shown experimentally with the -EST clone 8683614--that is prematurely truncated in the GenBank annotation. Furthermore, by combining coding constraints with spliced alignment, the algorithm rejects retained introns as found in the -EST clone 8689424. The RAFL cDNAs, described below, confirm that this was most likely a correct decision.
  3. The gene structure of At5g02810 as experimentally verified by cDNAs sequenced from the Riken Arabidopsis Full-Length (RAFL) cDNA project [3]. This experimental data supports the internal exon structure as predicted by Exdomino.
  4. A collection of - and -ESTs that demonstrate the case where, for a given gene, - and -EST evidence does not cover its internal region, producing ambiguity as to the gene's correct and complete exon structure.
Note: EST sequences were aligned using the GeneSeqer program [4]; see also http://www.plantgdb.org/AtGDB/.
