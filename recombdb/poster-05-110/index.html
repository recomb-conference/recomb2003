110.  Hierarchical Clustering of Protein Structures by using information of Multiple Structural Alignments

María Elena Ochagavía[1],[2] and Shoshana J. Wodak2

Keywords: Structure, Alignment, Protein, Superposition, Fold, Sub-structure, Clustering

1 Introduction

Multiple structural alignments methods have been used for detailed structural and functional analysis of protein families, in order to determine conserved residues useful in comparative modeling of related proteins and to propose hypothesis about their origins and evolutionary history.

Recently, we have developed a new multiple protein structural alignment method (MALECON) that maximizes the number of spatially equivalent residues [1]. Our approach uses as starting point a set of pair wise structural alignments and produces several multiple structural alignments, which are most consistent with this set, and where the proteins are optimally superimposed. This set of alternative solutions can facilitate the detailed study of both, the conserved residues and the structural variations in a protein family. The algorithm is not forced to always produce at least one multiple structural alignment for the entire set of proteins. If the global consistency is below the level specified by the user, multiple alignments are produced only for subsets of the proteins.

In this works we show how the ability of MALECON to produce many alternative solutions for multiple alignments of protein structures can be used to perform a clustering analysis of protein structures with the same architecture but with different topology. The clustering results obtained by using information from multiple structural alignments are compared with those obtained by a more standard approach based on pair wise structural similarities.

Clustering protein structures using information on pair wise structural alignments.

Given a set of protein structures p1,..,pn, the distance d(pi,pj) was calculated as the rms deviation divided by the number of spatial equivalent residues obtained after superposition of pi and pj using SOFIST [3] program. The distance matrix was generated by applying d to all pair of proteins. The clustering procedure was done by using the neighbor joining method as implemented in the PHYLIP [2] package.

3 Results and Conclusions

To illustrate our method we applied the two clustering procedures described above to a set of 16 protein structures adopting 2 different folds. Eight of these adopt the OB fold (PDB codes: 1csp, 1bovA, 1lttD, 1cuk, 1j5E, 1asyA, 1a0i and 1ey0A) and a further 8 adopt the SH3 fold (PDB codes: 1shg, 1ihvA, 1d0zA, 1bia, 1vie, 1dj7B, 1psf, 1whi). These folds have the same architecture but differ in their topology.  As can be observed in Figure 1A, our procedure achieves a perfect grouping of the proteins belonging to each fold. On the other hand the more traditional method (Figure 1B), which uses the matrix of pairwise structural alignments is unable to classify correctly 1ey0A and 1d0zA. In particular, the first structure, reported as a SH3 fold, is found to be the closest structure to 1shg, reported as OB fold.

This preliminary result indicates that the information on multiple alignments can be more informative than that obtained from pair wise alignments. This approach should therefore, be useful in clustering and classification of proteins structures, especially for difficult cases, were very similar pairwise structural similarity scores are obtained for different pairs of structures.
