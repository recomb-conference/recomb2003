48. Multiple Local Sequence Structure Alignment of unaligned RNA Sequences


 Sven Siebert, 1 Rolf Backofen 2

Keywords: multiple alignment,rna,local,sequence structure

Introduction

We present a new method that finds and aligns local sequence structure segments in a set of unaligned RNA sequences of unknown structures. Recognizing secondary structural features allows us to find highly probable sequence structure segments with high similarities among them. Given a local structure hint, e.g. a stem loop, structures are generated around this starting structure. All generated structures are searched for in RNA sequences fitting to their sequence segments. Occurrence probabilities and pairwise similarity scores are computed for the multiple alignment step which is done by the program T-Coffee. It has been shown that our results coincide mostly with experimental results.

Algorithm

The method is a five step algorithm, of which the input consists of n unaligned RNA sequences and a structure hint given in bracket notation (e.g. ``((...))''). The output is a multiple alignment of local sequence structure segments out of the n unaligned RNA sequences. This algorithm is characterized by mainly two features, namely in searching local sequence structure segments fulfilling
  • highly probable occurrences of sequence structure segments and
  • maximizing local sequence structure similarity among all RNA sequences.

Multiple local sequence structure alignment
Structures are generated around the structure hint with an edit distance (of at most constant d). It is a sum of weights of the edit operations insertion of a base, deletion of a base and breaking a base pair into two bases. These local structures are searched for in the RNA sequences. Two additional parameters qualify a local sequence structure segment for each RNA sequence. On the one hand, structure occurrence probabilities are computed by slight modifications of the McCaskill's base pair probability equation[1]. On the other hand, pairwise similarity scores as proposed by Zhang[3] ensure sequence structure similarities. These final segments can be seen as RNAs on their own, such that multiple alignment is done by the program T-COFFEE[4] from Higgins.

Test data

Our sequences are taken from SELEX (Selective Evolution of Ligands by EXponential enrichment) experiments. These primary mRNA sequences are proved to contain nearly common local sequence structure motifs by experiments. They form a stem loop like structure called selenocysteine incorporation sequence (SECIS) after the UGA stop codon. They are recognized by the special elongation factor SelB, which is required for the incorporation of the 21st amino acid Selenocysteine into proteins. In fig.1, five mRNA sequences from SELEX experiments are given. Structures similar to ``((((.....))))'' are searched for in these sequences. Best local structures are assigned to their sequences with respect to occurrence probabilities and sequence structure similarities. These results coincide with experimental results.
