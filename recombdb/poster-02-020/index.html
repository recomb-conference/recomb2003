20. Tabu search method for isothermic DNA sequencing by hybridization1


Jacek Bazewicz23, Piotr Formanowicz23, Marta Kasprzak23, Wojciech T. Markiewicz3, Aleksandra Swiercz2



Keywords: sequencing by hybridization, isothermic libraries, heuristics, tabu search

Introduction.

In this work, a problem of isothermic DNA sequencing by hybridization (SBH) is considered. The classical approach to SBH uses sets of oligonucleotides of equal lengths [1,7,9,5,8,6]. The isothermic version of the SBH method uses sets of oligonucleotides of equal temperatures (in fact, melting temperatures of oligonucleotide duplexes) but different lengths [2,3]. Every nucleotide adds its increment to the oligonucleotide temperature and it is widely assumed, that A or T gives 2 degrees and C or G gives 4 degrees. It has been proved, that two isothermic libraries that differ in their temperatures by 2 degrees are sufficient to cover any DNA sequence. The hybridization experiment using isothermic libraries is expected to provide data with a lower number of errors than the classical approach, due to the similarity of melting temperatures. As a result, reconstructed sequences are usually much more similar to the original ones.

The algorithm.

From the computational point of view, the problem of isothermic DNA sequencing with errors in input data is hard, similarly to its classical counterpart [4]. Hence, there is a need for developing heuristic algorithms providing good suboptimal solutions. In this work, a heuristic algorithm based on the tabu search approach is proposed. The algorithm solves the problem with both positive and negative errors. Results of the computational experiment, shown in Tables 1 and 2, prove a high quality of the proposed method.

In the tables, 600-20%+20% means, that to the ideal data without errors, created on the base of sequences of length 600 nucleotides, 20% of random negative errors and additionally 20% of random positive errors have been introduced (i.e. 120+120=240 errors in total). The values presented in the table are average for 10 coding sequences taken from GenBank. Usage is the percentage of correct oligonucleotides used in building solutions, while similarity shows how close the original and the reconstructed sequences are. The first oligonucleotide in an original sequence has been assumed to be known.
