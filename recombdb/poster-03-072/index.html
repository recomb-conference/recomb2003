72. In Silico Prediction of transcription factors that cooperate with E2F

Sheng Zhong[1],  Qing Zhou[2],  Wing Hung Wong2

Keywords: transcription factor, binding site module, cluster, E2F

1     Introduction

At sequences level, eukaryotic gene expressions are usually controlled by regulatory modules rather than isolated single transcription factor biding sites (TFBS) [1]. Berman et al introduced an approach to utilize TFBS clustering information to discover cis-regulatory modules in Drosophila genome [2]. However, according to our knowledge, there are few successful mammalian module discovery exercises being described. This is mainly due to the large number of mammalian transcription factors and the high false positive rate of predicted TFBSs. We show that by starting from a promoter set of pre-collected responsive genes of a given TF, it is possible to computationally predict its interacting partners with high sensitivity and specificity.

The E2F family of transcription factors is essential for the timely activation of genes involved in DNA replication and cell cycle control, exerting both positive and negative effects on gene expression [3]. However, it has been shown that the presence of an E2F site is not sufficient for G1/S phase transcriptional regulation. Some transcription factors can interact with E2F and activate promoters in a synergistic manner. Such interactions stabilize DNA binding and contribute to promoter-specificity. In this poster we use E2F as an example to demonstrate a computational approach in identifying transcription factors that can cooperate with a given transcription factor to either activate or repress gene expression. Our algorithm reported 15 such transcription factors, of which, 4 (TFE3, YY1, “ccaat” binding protein, P53) have been proven to activate or repress promoters together with E2F in a synergistic manner; 2(YY1, E box binding protein/Myc) are supported by ChIP results; 2(RAR, Myb) have suggestive evidence from hybrid screens with E2F proteins [4] [5] [6].

2     Method

332 human and mouse promoters of E2F target genes are collected as training set. 2000 randomly selected promoters are used as control. Every promoter covers1000 nt upstream and 50 nt down­stream to Transcription Start Site (TSS). We downloaded all (342) vertebrate position specific score matrices (PSSM) from Transfac. We applied the Match [7] program to scan every upstream sequence for PSSM calls.

To reduce false positive calls, we looked for clusters of PSSM calls. For a given PSSM, a clus­ter is defined by a significant grouping of its calls, on any training upstream sequence, comparing to all possible groupings of calls of the same PSSM on all control upstream sequences.

We introduced a sliding window scheme to find the groupings of PSSM calls on all control sequences. The window size to be 300 nt. The window is shifted by 100 nt in each step of the scanning process. For every PSSM, every time the window slid, we calculated the number of calls that were covered by the window. After the window slid through all control sequences one by one, we added up the number of windows that covered the same number of calls. Thus we got a “background grouping distribution” for this particular PSSM. Such background grouping distribu­tion was calculated for every PSSM.

For every PSSM, setting the p value to be 0.0005, we calculated a critical value (CV) for its groupings of calls. The CV was defined that less than 0.0005 of the groupings having equal to or more than CV calls among all sliding windows.

We used the same sliding window system to scan the training promoters. Fixing a PSSM, if any one window contained more than or equal to CV calls, we would identify the calls within this window as a cluster. We identified all clusters for all PSSMs, and grouped together the PSSMs for the same transcription factor. We identified all the transcription factors that had clusters on two or more promoters, including at least one human promoter, in the training set.

3     Result and discussion

15 transcription factors with 40 clusters were identified. These transcription factors are highly likely to activate or repress promoters with E2F in a synergistic manner. Our algorithm gave the cluster and a closest putative E2F binding site as a module.

By searching literature, we have found 8 transcription factors, which have been verified at various levels to activate or repress promoters synergisticly with E2F family factors. As discussed in the introduction section, 7 of them showed up in our 15 reported transcription factors. Only one transcription factor, DMP1[8], was missed by our algorithm. This was due to the lack of a PSSM for DMP1 in Transfac.

Therefore we believe our algorithm can serve as a good guidance in looking for E2F’s cooperative partners. It would be interesting if in vitro or in vivo experiments can be done to test the modules given by our algorithm. We also see that our approach is limited by the fact that not all transcription factors have PSSM compiled for their binding sites. Future work could first apply motif finding software like Bioprospector 9 to harvest novel PSSMs and combine those PSSMs with PSSMs provided by Transfac. Then feed all those PSSMs into our algorithm.
