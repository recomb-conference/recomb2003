145. Estimating the score distributions of induced and non-induced genes in microarray experiments


Stefanie Scheid 1 and Rainer Spang 1



Keywords: score distribution, mixture, induced genes, False Discovery Rate

Introduction

A microarray experiment measures simultaneously the abundance of mRNA for several thousands of genes in a given tissue. With two classes of tissue or state of patient one can search for genes that show differential gene expression with respect to the classes. Based on the microarray data we assign a Wilcoxon ranksum score for differential gene expression to every gene. Typically a high score corresponds to overexpression and a low score to underexpression. A gene showing differential gene expression is called ``induced''.

The score distributions of microarray experiments are mixtures of score distributions of induced and non-induced genes. The two distributions can be distinguished if the truly induced genes show very high (or low resp.) scores. However, in a typical setting the two distributions overlap and only highly induced genes can be selected with common methods. The main part of the induced genes merge with non-induced genes in ``twilight zones''. It is a challenging problem to discover where twilight zones are and how many truly induced genes one can expect at a certain score level. If we observe a score for a specific gene we cannot only tell how deep it is situated in a twilight zone but also how likely it is to be an induced gene if the mixture is known at that score level.

Method

We introduce a new method that is based on the idea of minimal removal of genes such that the score distribution of remaining genes is similar to a score distribution of non-induced genes. The original score distribution is derived from two-class data. Distributions for non-induced genes are generated by class permutation. We divide the range of scores into intervals (bins) and count the relative bin frequencies of the permuted samples. For each bin a lower and upper quantile of relative bin frequency is calculated.

The basic idea is to remove genes until all relative bin frequencies of the remaining original scores are contained within the quantile intervals of the permutations. The remaining original scores therefore appear as being derived from non-induced genes. We estimate the two mixtures as the relative bin frequencies of the remaining scores for the non-induced and of the excluded scores for the induced part. Removing induced genes makes the two distributions more similar. However, removing non-induced genes in addition does not necessarily lead to more dissimilar distributions. Therefore, the central point is to exclude as few scores as possible in order to provide a conservative estimate for the number of induced genes.

To take advantage of the permutations' diversity we compare the original scores to each sample of permutation scores. In a first step the differences of their absolute bin frequencies are computed. One by one we exclude those genes whose original and permuted scores are redundant with respect to the differences. This selection stops if either all differences are zero or if no more suitable genes can be found.

In a second step we compare the relative bin frequencies of the remaining original scores to the quantile intervals and remove genes if necessary. If a bin contains a number too small to reach the lower quantile, excluded genes are included again. This procedure stops if either all relative bin frequencies fit into the quantile intervals or if no more suitable genes can be found.

We conduct the method with a high number of permutations and estimate the score distribution of non-induced genes as the mean relative bin frequencies of the remaining original scores over all permutations. In a simulation study we evaluate the new method under several settings of sparsity. The choice of the lower and upper quantile points and the number and design of the bin segmentation is a topic for further investigation.

Context to established approaches

Classical approaches of separation like estimating mixture models require knowledge of underlying distribution functions. A method that does not need this information is a variant of the recently introduced False Discovery Rate (FDR). In a multiple testing situation it estimates the proportion of genes that are falsely called induced among all genes called induced ([1], [2]). Used in the common way the FDR only touches the twilight zone because genes are called induced whose scores exceed or fall below a certain threshold.

If we define the FDR in an altered way and call all genes induced that fall into a specific bin, we can calculate a bin-wise FDR to estimate the score distribution of non-induced genes. The disadvantage of this method is the need of prior knowledge, i.e. an adequate estimate for the overall amount of non-induced genes. The commonly used estimator for the normal FDR as given in [3] will overestimate the amount of non-induced genes if the scores of induced genes are hidden among scores of non-induced genes.
