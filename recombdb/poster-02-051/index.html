51.  A New Method for Predicting Haplotype Pairs for Genotypes

Werner Terhalle[1], Willi Schmidt[2], Karla Köpke[3]

Keywords: haplotype predicition, genotype-phenotype relationship

1  Introduction

Now high throughput methods are given for screening DNA sequence variation in human populations to analyse interindividual differences in disease risk and treatment response. Up to date more than 1.4 million single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) were found; most genes contain multiple SNPs. Haplotypes as combinations of specific SNPs on one chromosome rather than single SNPs should be analysed to study the consequences of sequence variability for the individual phenotype. Because molecular haplotyping of genomic DNA is a laborious process, in silico methods for prediction of the haplotype pair underlying an individual genotype is a relevant step in analysing genotype-phenotype relationships.

2  Common methods

There are several methods for predicting haplotypes known in the literature. Each of these methods is based either on a parsimony principle that searches for a minimum set of haplotypes explaining a given sample of genotypes, or on a maximum-likelihood principle that tries to find frequencies for all possible haplotypes that maximize the likelihood of a given genotype sample.

Given a sample of genotypes, Clark's well-known algorithm [1] starts by resolving all unambiguous genotypes containing at most one heterozygous state, and then enlarges this set of haplotypes iteratively by subtracting haplotypes already known to be present in the population from genotypes not resolved so far. This algorithm has the disadvantages, that in some cases it does not explain  all genotypes (or it even can not start), and, furthermore, it is depending on the order of genotypes. The PHASE algorithm by Stephens et al. [2] improves this algorithm by inferring a statistical method for allowing mutations in the haplotypes added in the iteration step to avoid the problem of getting stuck.

Excoffier and Slatkin [3] have published an expectation-maximization (EM) algorithm to maximize the likelihood of the given sample of genotypes. Several algorithms exist refining this maximum-likelihood approach: Niu et al. [4] extended this algorithm by a divide-and-conquer approach to allow longer genotypes to be considered, and Rohde and Fuerst [5] include nuclear family information. While the maximum-likelihood methods are rather good for the estimation of haplotype frequencies in a population they are often weak in assigning haplotype pairs to individuals (although Rohdes and Fuersts algorithm is helpful for this); furthermore, they are based on the assumption of fulfilled Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium (HWE).

3  A new method

Especially in selected samples which contain diseased individuals a violation of HWE is likely. Therefore, we propose an algorithm that is independent of the assumption of HWE. Our method does not rely on parsimony nor on maximum likelihood principles. We assign a value to each possible haplotype which measures the importance of this particular haplotype in explaining the genotypes considered in the sample - a value which may be interpreted as the likelihood of this haplotype. Then, to each haplotype pair we simply assign the product of the two respective numbers resulting in a list of haplotype pairs for each genotype weighted according to their likelihood of being the pair underlying the particular genotype.

This new method

- is applicable for every sample of genotypes,

- does not assume Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium,

- lists for each genotype all possible haplotype pairs and a score for their plausibility

- is very fast.
