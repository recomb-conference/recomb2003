126. Modular Decision System and Information Integration for Cancer Outcome Prediction


Matthias E. Futschik, 1 Mike Sullivan, 2 Anthony Reeve, 3
Nikola Kasabov 4



Keywords: disease outcome prediction, gene expression, clinical information, modular decision model, information integration

Introduction

Prediction of clinical behaviour and treatment for cancers is based on the integration of clinical and pathologic parameters. Recent reports have demonstrated that gene expression profiling provides a powerful new approach for determining disease outcome [1,2,3]. If clinical and microarray data each contain independent information then it should be possible to combine these data sets to gain more accurate prognostic information. Here we have used existing clinical information and microarray data to generate a combined prognostic model which achieves a significant improvement in the accuracy of outcome prediction for diffuse large B-cell lymphoma (DLBCL).

Construction of prognostic modules

To test the hypothesis that clinical and microarray data contain independent information, we constructed two separate prognostic models for DLBCL using the clinical information and microarray data published recently by Shipp et al. [1]. For the clinical model, the hitherto standard International Prediction Index (IPI) for DLBCL was converted into a Bayesian classifier based on the five-year survival rates in the original IPI clinical study [4]. The clinical prognostic model achieved an accuracy of 73.2%.

For the microarray-based predictor, we used an evolving fuzzy neural network (EFuNN) classifier for adaptive supervised learning [5]. The microarray-based predictor achieved an accuracy of 78.5% which was slightly better than that of the previously used support vector machines (SVMs) [1].

Independence of modules

The calculation of the mutual information of the predictions by the two models (deriving ) shows the surprising result that the clinical prognostic model is statistically independent of the microarray-based predictor even though several of the IPI risk factors were considered biological surrogates of underlying molecular mechanisms and were expected to be correlated with the gene expression in the tumour [1,4].

Modular decision system

To exploit the complementary nature of the two prognostic models, we combined both predictors in a hierarchical modular prognostic model (Fig.1). Model parameters were optimized by error-backpropagation. Our combined model achieved an improved accuracy of 87.5%. An analysis of the combined model showed that both microarray data and clinical information were required for improved prediction accuracy. Stratification of the samples into clinical subgroups according to their IPI value revealed that the two predictor modules differed in their `areas of expertise'.
Figure: Three-layered hierarchical model for combination of clinical and microarray-based predictors: The first layer (Predictor module layer) consists of independently trained predictor modules; the second layer (Class unit layer) integrates the weighted predictions of both predictors for classes `cured'/`fatal'; the third layer (Decision layer) produces the final prediction based on the weighted sum of the outputs of class units.

Our study demonstrates for the first time (to our knowledge) that the integration of microarray data with previously established clinical parameters can considerably improve the disease outcome prediction.
