71. Bacterial RM-systems revisited using degenerate pattern consensi


Mathias Vandenbogaert 1



Keywords: restriction- and modification systems, word probabilities, IUPAC ambiguity codes, implementation, QuickScore-library, combinatorics.

Introduction

Recognition sites for type II RM enzymes in genomes of several bacteria are semi­-palindromes that are avoided at a significant degree, relating the avoidance of those short oligonucleotide words to RM-systems [3]. In this context, distinct words that are recognized are selected against, creating so-called ``contrast'' words. Bacteria that show a more stringent word usage bias cope with less selective pressure than those that have words of a more random composition. In this study, focusing on pattern analysis methods applied to semi­-palindromes in bacterial RM-systems, the correlation between contrasting individual 6-palindromes in genomic texts and the presence of RM enzymatic systems is being analysed systematically.

Experimental Results - Discussion

Statistical methods have been widely used in determining the level of over­- or under­-representation of contrast words [5,2,1] in the field of genomics. Our objective is to check whether the observed repertoire of avoided words according to various models, is reflecting homology from similarity as compared by BLAST protein comparisons only. In other terms, we wonder about the correspondance between the scope of avoided words in a microorganism, the similarity between RM-system enzymes for this organism, and eventually the degree of homology between them.

Considering degenerate palindromes in a genome, their significance has been assessed using word­-counting procedures, through a comparison against various models available in the QuickScore library [1]. The avoidance patterns we consider take into account the words included in the set of degenerate palindromes with retained symmetry. So far, the included patterns are those that allow at most 2 conventional ACGT letters. This has been done in order to avoid the blurring effect of intermingling -scores for 2- and 3-letter ambiguity codes.

As we are dealing with statistics that are admitting some error in the words (while respecting their palindromic symmetry), the list of avoided words sorted on ­-score shows similarity in the list­-tops, through comparison of taxonomically related genomes. The preservation of the rankings of the words, can be explained for organisms that are closely related by taxonomic branchings, by overall gene similarity, and hence avoidance of the same words, due to the presence of related RM-systems.

The same relation cannot be clearly made when we consider the similarity through Blast searches for RM proteic sequences. For example, although Bacillus and Listeria are part of the same taxonomic branch, the similarity for the sequences in both genomes is not striking.

As an example we state the proteins from L. monocytogenes that have some degree of similarity (not better than an -value of ) with proteins encoded by B. subtilis: M.BsuFI and M.BsuRI are similar with L. monocytogenes ppk1 gene (acc. AJ302030.1). Their recognition sequence is 5' CCGG 3' and 5' GGCC 3'; their prototypes are HpaII and HaeIII from Haemophilus aegyptius, respectively. The other way round, no Listeria similar proteins can be found in the Blast results for B. subtilis.

This means that although the fact a certain degree of similarity can be found among the recognition sequences for close genomes, this is only due to selective pressure on the recognition sequence, and that the rate of evolution of RMS sequences is higher than the point mutations that are allowed on the recognition sequence.

If we neglige the importance of being phylogenetically close, certain properties for well-determined genomic strains are brought to day-light. The used methodology is the following, when focussing on Listeria.

  • We investigate by a regular Blast to what orgnanism the sequences do belong that most resemble the RMS proteic sequences of Listeria.
  • We look up its recognition sequence from the ReBase Database for restriction systems [4], if known.
  • We compare the avoidance of patterns with degeneracy for Listeria and this orgnanism. It is very likely that a variant of the former mentioned recognition sequence will be shared by both organisms.

This way, as an exerpt from our results, we see that for the genome of Mycobacterium tuberculosis, when looking at the best candidates through similarity according to Blast, a lot of E. coli sequences can be found, and the best are encoded in H. influenzae sd.

Conclusion and perspectives

This study extends previous analyses undertaken at establishing phylogenetic relationships, starting from contrast words, with respect to RM-systems present in specific clades. The model used in this study is able to reflect related RMS in closely related taxa, and can reveal events that are classified as mediated by horizontal gene transfers (HGT). The use of IUPAC ambiguity codes, eventually supplemented with information about divergent evolutionary rates among RMS families of proteins, will eventually give a better and more refined understanding of genome organization, interaction and dynamics of evolution mechanisms through mobile elements, like transposons and plasmids.
