2. Visualization of the genome-wide haplotype structure in mice using SNPview

Serge Batalov1, Tim Wiltshire1, Mathew T. Pletcher1,2, Colin F. Fletcher[1],2

Keywords: haplotype, diversity, SNP, SSLP, marker, QTL, genotyping, mouse.

1   Introduction

The laboratory mouse is an excellent experimental model, in part because of well-defined strain genealogies, standardized mapping tools, and the availability of sophisticated genetic technologies. Simple sequence-length polymorphic (SSLP) markers have, until now, provided the foundation for genetic mapping in the mouse but the use of single-nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) is an attractive option because they are by far the most abundant variation in the genome [1,2], potentially providing a much larger marker set.

        To identify a genome-wide panel of SNPs, we selected 2,600 evenly distributed loci (STSs) for PCR amplification and sequencing from 6 common and well characterized inbred ("laboratory") mouse strains (C57BL/6J, 129S1/SvImJ, C3H/HeJ, DBA/2J, A/J, BALB/cByJ) and two inbred strains derived from wild mice, M. m. castaneus and M. spretus. This approach provides complete allele information and thus allows simultaneous comparison of all strains. A significant challenge is the visualization of the data.

        These data were incorporated with publicly available data [1,3] to form a database containing 18,000+ individual SNPs across 3950 loci. After mapping these SNPs to the assembled mouse genome [4], we developed a Web based interface to the database (see below) to examine the distribution of polymorphisms between strains. Pairwise comparison of C57BL/6J with each of the other laboratory strains in this dataset revealed 23 non-polymorphic regions greater than 20 Mb; the largest 'gap' is 61 Mb on chromosome 10 between C57BL/6J and DBA/2J. Two regions averaging over 20 Mb, on chromosomes 10 and 13, are completely deficient in polymorphisms in all five combinations.

        We have extended this analysis to all strain comparisons by identifying regions of shared haplotype. Thus, regions containing a series of identical alleles between two or more strains are indicated as blocks of identical color in those strains. Shared haplotypes were defined by 1) Identifying the longest regions of contiguous strain pair identity; 2) Taking those two strain pairs, and all other strains bearing the same alleles, and coloring them as shared haplotypes within that region; 3) Where alleles diverged, or, any different allele pairings occurred within a longest block, they were identified with different colors, the same allele groups bearing the same colors.

        Regions of haplotype sharing were in some cases surprisingly large, up to 120Mb, where no SNPs were identified between strain pairs by our sampling methods. In most regions no more than three haplotypes were identified for a given interval, suggesting that common laboratory mouse strains represent a genetic mosaic of a smaller number of founder strains.

        These results indicate that large, but discrete regions of the genome are not very polymorphic between particular strain pairs, and thus cannot easily be interrogated by SNPs or SSLPs for natural genetic variations influencing QTLs. A comprehensive survey of variation allows the identification of strains pairs that share the fewest haplotypes. To that end, we have typed 21 additional strains with a smaller set of 934 SNP assays to determine the extent of strain diversity.

2    GNF SNP database and SNPview.

A Web-based, fully searchable database was developed containing sequence data and allele information across 8 inbred strains for all SNP loci. We incorporated publicly available SNPs that were useful in a C57BL/6J strain combination, 2757 SNPs from the Whitehead Institute SNP data (http://www-genome.wi.mit.edu/SNP/mouse) and 296 SNPs from the Roche database (http://mouseSNP.roche.com).

        SNPview, the interactive navigator for the individual SNPs, SSLPs, alleles and haplotypes projected to the genomic axis is available on-line at http://www.gnf.org/SNP/ . Several different views are available for each chromosome: a "chromatogram" view of SNPs, major and minor alleles, haplotypes, and combinations of the above. The data for the selected strains is displayed at the full chromosomes or user-defined intervals, along with the reference tracks of the LocusLink gene loci, publicly available SSLP markers, and the subset of SSLP markers informative for the two selected strains. The choice of the Celera or Ensembl genomic coordinates is available. DerBrowser [5] is used as a tool to present the genomic mapping for ease of SNP, SSLP and haplotype comparison. DerBrowser features scrolling, zooming, text labels and hyperlinks to the original and external data.

3       Conclusions

Will a very dense set of SNPs be required to define haplotypes, as suggested by studies in the human? Our study says no. For many other plant and animal strains that have population histories similar to the mouse, we can expect that a much smaller sampling of SNPs will define genome haplotypes.

        Can the mouse still be used as an experimental model to understand the evolutionary forces that affect haplotypes? Yes - in fact our study provides the needed framework for designing and interpreting those experiments. As one example, this study identifies genomic regions that can be interrogated to define mutation rates in different genetic backgrounds.

        This study provides a framework in the mouse for investigating the mechanisms that underlie haplotype conservation and the extent of linkage disequilibrium, as well as a powerful toolset for exploiting this knowledge to clone mouse genes from mutant or quantitatively variant strains. The genotyping and haplotype analysis for 24 additional strains is currently under way.
