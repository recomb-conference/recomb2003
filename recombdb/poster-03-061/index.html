61.  Detection of Regulatory Elements
by Comparative DNA Analysis

Ulrike Gausmann[1],  Niels Jahn1,  Cornelia Platzer[2],
Karol Szafranski1,  Matthias Platzer1

 

Keywords: promoter, transcription factor binding sites, comparative sequence analysis

1     Introduction

Within the SFB 604 ‘Multi­functional Signaling Proteins’ (http://www.imb-jena.de/SFB604/) we deal with proteins which physically interact and may be potentially co-regulated.  Comparative analysis of the 5' upstream gene regions should reveal regulatory elements common for all members of a group because functional elements are more strictly conserved and can be identified by align­ment procedures.  Sensitive pairwise align­ment algorithms are the first steps but will not give clear results in many cases.  For some purposes specia­lized programs can be used, e.g. ‘DNA block aligner’ (DBA) for the detection of conserved blocks in human and rodent orthologous non-coding sequences [1].

2        Comparative promoter analysis of orthologs, paralogs, and co‑regulated genes

The most common method for the prediction of putative regulatory regions in candidate sequences is based on the comparison of the nucleotide sequence with known binding matrices for transcription factor binding sites (TFBS), e.g. with MatInspector or MATCH.  However, these predictions still appear to be insufficient in sensitivity and specificity.  Several improvements are on the way, namely data extension for matrix computation (TransFac), building of higher order binding site models (e.g. TransCompel), and the consideration of comparative approaches (e.g. rVISTA or TOUCAN).

Pairwise comparisons were done with several local align­ment programs and best results were obtained for orthologs from man and rodents with DBA (see below).  Analyses of more than two corresponding regions was performed with the HGFR gene (hepatocyte growth factor receptor or c‑met) where twelve genomic sequences (main source  http:// www.nisc.nih.gov/ projects/ zooseq.html) can be assembled so far.  This gene contains no TATA box, but is associated with a CpG-island and seems to have a non-coding first exon separated by more than 20 kb from the second one.  Reasonable alignments (BlastZ) were obtained with ten mammalian sequences after repeat-masking.  50 kb of non-coding sequence upstream from the startcodon was used in each case.  Regions with high similarity were extracted and analyzed with TFBS predicting programs.  The results are compared with the output of DBA analyses of rodent and human sequences.

As a representative of a set of paralogs we are investigating potassium channels where the subunit compositions are tissue dependent.  Details will be shown at the conference.

3     The IL‑10 gene as an example for comparative analysis of orthologs

As an example for the applicability of the comparative approach we present the analysis of the Interleukin‑10 (IL‑10) promoter.  IL‑10 is a cytokine with immuno­suppressive and anti-inflammatory properties and its synthesis can be induced or enhanced by various stimuli, particularly via a cAMP-dependent signaling pathway.  Two functionally active CRE motifs, CRE1 and CRE4, were identified in the promoter region previously.  It was suspected that other, non-CRE sites play also a role in this path­way.  Comparative analysis of three orthologous promoter sequences (H. sapiens, M. musculus, M. monax) with DBA and MatInspector revealed that two binding sites for the CCAAT enhancer binding protein, C/EBP3 and 5, and the previously identified CRE4 site, are located in conserved regions.  Using EMSA and reporter gene assays the prominent functional role of these sites was experimentally confirmed.  In contrast, the weakly conserved sites CRE1 and C/EBP1 were shown to be less important [2].

4     Further research directions

The IL-10 example demonstrates the reasonability of close collaboration between bio­informa­ticians and experimental researchers.  As the number of known genomes increases the back­ground modeling of genomic sequences from different species will be more reliable and therefore, multiple align­ment procedures will result in more significant predictions for non-coding DNA regions.  Some approaches aim for analyses that can be performed with lengthy sequences where programs like DBA need too much computing time [3]. In the ‘Jenaer Centre of Bioinformatics’ (http:// www.imb-jena.de/ jcb/) we are evaluating parameters for align­ment and prediction programs and are developing an analysis pipeline for sequence datasets.

The evaluation and optimization of algorithms requires test- and training sets and the automated data transfer from local storage to analysis programs.  Hence, we started to build a relational database which will also provide the experimental researchers within the SFB with data they need for their experiments, i.e. sequences with corresponding annotations derived from public references and from our analyses.  To enhance the data basis we are including paralogous and orthologous gene sequences.
