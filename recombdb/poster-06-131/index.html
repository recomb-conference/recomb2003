131. Feature reduction and classification of pancreatic cancer vs healthy pancreas based on gene expression data

Hans A. Kestler[1][2], Malte Bucholz2, Wolfgang Böck2, Friedhelm Schwenker1,
Andre Müller1,2, Günther Palm1, Thomas M. Gress2

The differentiation between cancerous and benign (non-cancerous) processes in the pancreas often poses a difficult diagnostic problem in the clinical setting while being of major importance for the treatment of patients. Since cancer development is caused by the accumulation of complex genetic alterations in the affected cells and tissues, the differentiation of cancerous vs. non-cancerous clinical samples is an important application –together with feature reduction methods– for the interpretation of DNA array data.

Gene expression data have two dimensions, genes on one side, and measurements on the other side. While the expression level of several thousands of genes can be measured in a single experiment, only a few dozens of experiments are normally carried out, leading to datasets of very high dimensionality and low cardinality. In this situation, feature reduction techniques capable of reducing the dimensionality of data are essential for building predictive tools based on classification.

In this study we used gene expression data collected at the University of Ulm from pancreatic cancer, healthy pancreas and chronic pancreatitis tissues using a DNA chip containing 486 features preselected for their known or suspected involvement in cancerogenesis1,2,3. The 486 features were reduced to 200. Nine features (genes) were removed from the analysis following visual inspection of the filter hybridization images because of overshining neighbouring signals. The remaining data set was filtered to include only genes for which normalized signal intensities were >= 0.8 in at least 5 of 26 samples. The samples were separated into two groups, 15 from pancreatic cancer tissue and eleven form healthy pancreas or chronic pancreatitis tissues.



The feature reduction step was performed by performing a principal component analysis. Figure 1 shows the eigenvalues of the covariance matrix sorted in decreasing order. Based on these results the first ten principal components were chosen for the subsequent analysis. Figure 2 shows the projection of the 26 samples onto the first two principal components.


Receiver operator characteristic (ROC) curves show the sensitivity (true-positive rate) against the 1-specificity (false-positive rate), they are used to assess the diagnostic value of tests depending on a single cut-off value of a continuous variable. The most commonly used quantitative global index summarizing the information provided by this curve is the area under it. A large area indicates a good discriminative ability of the marker with a single cut-off value. Su and Liu4 have shown that Fisher's linear discriminant maximizes sensitivity uniformly over the entire range of specificity when the two distributions are assumed normal with proportional covariance matrices. This linear combination also generates a new virtual diagnostic marker and can be regarded as a perceptron training procedure in two phases (a) determining the direction of the hyperplane and (b) setting the threshold. We used ROC curves to assess the diagnostic ability of all linear combinations (1023) of the first ten principal components (pc) and ranked the resulting virtual markers in terms of their area under the ROC curve. Figure 3 shows the best singular pc, and Figure 4 the best linear combination with the least number of features (pc 1, 2, and 4). Compared to the already remarkable 92% accuracy on the first pc (see Figure 2 and 3), the 96% accuracy on the linear combination of three principal components (Figure 4) seem even more promising.
