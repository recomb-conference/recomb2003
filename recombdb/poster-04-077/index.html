77. Classification of species based on DNA style

Charles Chapus[1], Bernard Fertil1, Scott Edwards[2], Alain Giron1, Patrick Deschavanne1

Keywords: genome, genomic signature, species classification, phylogeny

1     Introduction

The recent availability of complete genomes opens a new field of research devoted to the general analysis of their global structure without regard to gene interpretation. The exploration of such large sequences requires a new kind of data analysis. We have shown that the whole set of oligonucleotide frequencies of a sequence is species specific [1]. DNA fragments conserved the signature of the species they come from [2] and such analysis may therefore be considered as a genomic signature. As the signature is very species specific, it can be used to classify species on its mere basis. The distance between images may quantify phylogenetic proximity. In this poster we will first search for the minimal length of DNA which could be used as reference for species signature. In a second step, we will classify species on a taxonomic basis and establish phylogenies based on DNA style.

2     Methods and Results

Seventy-four complete genomes belonging to the three domains of life were investigated. Comparison of DNA fragment signatures to that of the whole genome showed that fragments as short as 1 kb conserved the species signature. This conservation is very good for prokaryotes and of less quality for eukaryotes due to intrinsic intra-genomic variations. However, when using partial genome sequence as reference, two variability factors are added, that from the fragment to classify and that from the one representing the species. Analysis of the classification result as a function of the two parameters (length of tested fragment and length of reference fragment) have shown that for reference sequence representing at least 10 % of the genome, the classification quality is quite similar to that obtained with the complete genome as reference. For reference fragment of 5 Kb, about 75% of 2.5 kb test fragments are correctly classified. This supports the previous hypothesis of a species-specific DNA style and allows to compare genomic DNA fragments of different size found in databases for species classification.

We established the signature of about 10 000 species of the 3 domains of life with the following criterion:

1/ DNA must be genomic (no mRNA, mitochondrial or chloroplast sequences)

2/ 1.5 kb as minimum size for DNA sequence incorporated for establishing species signature

The resulting size of DNA sequences classified varies from 1.5 kb to 32 MB. In a first step, a leave-one-out discriminant classification was done in descending order of taxonomic complexity (fig 1). Signature distinguishes very well between prokaryotes and eukaryotes as well as bacteria from archea. Within the eukarya domain, the separation is of lesser quality but with rather good results (fig 1). This allows proposing the use sequence signature to explore phylogenetic relationships. We have already show that very good phylogenetic trees were obtained when using homologous sequence signature[3]. We study the place of flightless birds (ratites) in the bird tree. With using non-homologous sequences of different sizes, varying from 1.5 to 600 kb, we have shown that the traditional hypothesis of basal ratites and monophyly of non-ratites birds (neognaths) was strongly supported by our analysis. This encouraging result leads us to explore the phylogeny of prokaryotes using complete genome signatures.
