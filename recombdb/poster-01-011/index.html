11. Prediction of Protein Function with THEMATICS

Mary Jo Ondrechen[1], Leonel F. Murga1, James G. Clifton[2], Dagmar Ringe2

Keywords: electrostatics, functional genomics, function prediction, THEMATICS, titration

1     Introduction

In the wake of genome sequencing, structural genomics initiatives are rapidly discovering thousands of new protein structures; many of these structures are of unknown function. In order to realize the many untold benefits of genome sequencing, a next important step is to discover the function of these gene products. We recently have reported [1, 2] on THEMATICS (Theoretical Microscopic Titration Curves), a new method for the prediction of protein function from structure based on established computational tools. In the present paper we review the method for obtaining the theoretical titration curves, then show how a small fraction of these calculated curves have anomalous shapes. A group of such anomalous residues that form a cluster in coordinate space is a reliable predictor of active site location. As examples, we show results for chorismate mutase from Bacillus subtilus, colicin E3 from E. coli, and D5-3-ketosteroid isomerase from Pseudomonas putida.

2     Methods

THEMATICS requires the atomic coordinates of the protein as input. First one solves for the electrical potential function using a Finite Difference Poisson Boltzmann (FDPB) procedure, followed by a Monte Carlo method to calculate the ensemble-average net charge C for each of the ionizable residues (Arg, Asp, Cys, Glu, His, Lys, Tyr and the N- and C- termini) as functions of the pH [3-7]. The next step is to analyze the predicted titration curves to select the ones with anomalous shape. It is found that most of the ionizable residues in a protein obey the Henderson-Hasselbalch (H-H) equation. The C(pH) curves, according to the H-H equation, are sigmoidal with a sharp fall-off in charge in a narrow pH range near the pKa. (The pKa is defined as the value for the pH at which exactly one-half of the protein molecules in the ensemble have that residue in charged form.) We have found [1, 2, 8] that a small fraction of the residues (about 3-7%) in an enzyme have titration curves that deviate substantially from the predicted sigmoidal form.

We classify all of the ionizable residues in a given protein as having either typical, sigmoidal titration curves or anomalous titration curves. For most proteins, this can be done with simple visual inspection. In order to screen large numbers of proteins, we have developed two different automated procedures to perform this classification on the calculated titration curves. One of these classifiers involves fitting the calculated curves to a parametrized sigmoid function, followed by statistical analysis. The second classifier utilizes machine learning technologies. Finally, the protein structure is searched to find clusters of anomalous residues in physical proximity. We report the list of anomalous residues, with clusters in proximity shown inside square brackets. For previously characterized proteins such as those shown here, known active site residues are given in boldface.

3        Results

Chorismate mutase is a vital enzyme in the biosynthesis of aromatic amino acids and it catalyzes the rearrangement of chorismate to prephenate via a Claisen rearrangement. It has a-b plaits topology and is active as a trimer. The calculations were performed on the trimer from the PDB structure 1DBF (from Bacillus subtilus) [9]. THEMATICS finds three equivalent active sites, one located at each of the three trimer interfaces. The THEMATICS result may be written as:

Chorismate mutase / 1DBF                    [C75, E78, R7, R63, Y108], [E19], [E34]

Here C75, E78, R7, R63, and Y108 are all known active site residues and form a cluster in physical space. E19 and E34 are each isolated in space and are considered to be isolated false positives.

Colicin E3 is an E. coli nuclease with a unique fold. The calculation was performed on a 3.0 Å resolution structure of the catalytic domain only, obtained from Professor Menachem Shoham. We obtained THEMATICS results prior to the completion of the biochemical characterization and thus we did not know where the active site should be. THEMATICS gave an unambiguous answer:

          Colicin E3                                            [E517, H526, R495, R545, Y519]

These five residues form a cluster around a cleft in the structure. This has been shown to be the correct active site [10].

      D5-3-Ketosteroid isomerase catalyzes the allylic isomerization of a double bond in a ketosteroid and has a cystatin B fold. Calculations were performed on the PDB structure 1OPY [11] of the protein from Pseudomonas putida. THEMATICS obtained the following results:

                D5-3-ketosteroid isomerase / 1OPY                [D103, Y16, Y32, Y57]

Again THEMATICS finds the correct active site unambiguously.

      Of the several dozen proteins we have studied to date, THEMATICS has proved to be a reliable predictor of active site location. The false positives tend to be isolated in space, such that a cluster of two or more anomalous residues in physical proximity predicts the active site.
