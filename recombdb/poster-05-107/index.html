107.  Common Structural Cliques: Efficient Algorithm for Automatic Extraction of Local Similarities from Protein Structures

Mariusz Milik[1], Sándor Szalma[2] and Krzysztof Olszewski[3]

Keywords: protein structure, structural templates, functional convergence, enzymes

1     Introduction

Proteins sharing common biological functions often have common local structural features. The reason for it is that in many cases protein function is determined by chemical character and spatial arrangement of few selected atoms. Looking at protein structures from the functional perspective, one may distinguish two classes of protein residues: (i) functionally relevant residues that participate in the specific protein function, and (ii) scaffold residues that form structural environment for the functional residues, keeping them in proper spatial configuration. The algorithm presented here allows for automatic selection of potential functionally relevant residues by analysis of local similarities in different structures of proteins belonging to the same functional family. The presented work is a modification and expansion of the previously published “FAUST” algorithm for extraction of functionally relevant templates from protein structures [1].

2     Methods

The presented search method is based on a comparison of local sub-structures (atom cliques) of proteins sharing a common biological function, and does not depend on overall similarity of the compared protein structures and sequences, or on the previous knowledge about functionally relevant residues in the considered protein family.

The Common Structural Clique (CSC) establishes correspondence between two compact subsets of atoms (atom cliques) selected from two protein structures. The correspondence is defined in such way that all inter-atomic distances between atoms belonging to the CSC are approximately equal in both protein structures, and both atoms belonging to the same pair have the same chemical identity. In the presented study, structures of proteins are represented by selected atoms from amino acid side chains, because we are interested in protein structures with enzymatic function. For other application of our CSC algorithm, other criteria may be used for choice of representative set of atoms.

The CSC procedure for structural template extraction may be summarized in three main steps. First, the graph representations of two compared protein structures are created and all possible four-atom cliques are extracted from these graphs. Second, the extracted cliques are exhaustively searched for CSC’s – pairs of isomorphic atom cliques, where first clique is extracted from first structure and the other one from the second protein structure. In the third step, extracted CSCs are filtered and combined in order to create a common sub-graph of two graphs representing protein structures. The goal of this stage of the procedure is to choose atoms most likely to be related to the common function of the compared proteins.

3     Results

Performance of the algorithm was tested on four structures of serine proteases: (i) 1A0J – trypsin, E.C. number 3.4.21.4, (ii) 1C5Y - urokinase-type plasminogen activator, E.C. number 3.4.21.73, (iii) 1BH6 – subtilisin, E.C. number 3.4.21.62, (iv) 1IC6 – proteinase K, E.C. number 3.4.21.64. Two of these proteins (1A0J and 1C5Y) belong to the trypsin family and two others (1BH6 and 1IC6) to the subtilisin family. The catalytic activity of the serine proteases from these families is provided by a charge relay system involving an aspartic acid residue hydrogen-bonded to a histidine, which itself is hydrogen-bonded to a serine (catalytic triad). The sequence around the residues involved in the catalytic triad of the subtilisin family proteins is completely different from that of the analogous residues in the trypsin family and most probably evolved by independent convergent evolution.

In this test we applied our automatic procedure for structural template extraction to three pairs of these proteins. First two pairs contained proteins from the same structural families, the third pair contained one protein from the trypsine and one protein from the subtilisin family.
