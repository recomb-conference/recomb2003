53. Predicting the functions of ABC-transporters from phylogenetic analysis


Holger Wagner 1



Keywords: Phylogenetic analysis, multilpe sequence alignments, ABC-transporters, Sinorhizobium meliloti, physiological function, sequence evolution, repeats



ABC-transporters [10] are a class of active permeases, where `active' means that the substrate transport is not restricted to diffusion, but can also be performed in direction of the concentration gradient. Such an active transport requires energy. ABC-transporters use the decomposition of ATP as source of energy, which particularly implies the name ATP binding cassette- (ABC-)transporter. In this study, we will use `ABC-transporter' as a designation for the complete aminoacid-sequences of the The physiological functions of ABC-transporters are given by the transported substrates, for example sugar, iron or aminoacids, together with the direction of the transport, namely import or export.

In this study, we investigate the ABC-transporters of sinorhizobium meliloti, a member in the family of rhizobium bacteria. The symbiosis between rhizobia and plants play an important rle for the fixation of nitrogen, which is an essential factor for plant growth. Over 50 % of the nitrogen fixed by living organisms is due to the symbiosis between rhizobia and plants, which makes it an interesting possibility to improve plant growth.

One of the most studied and best characterized rhizobia is sinorhizobium meliloti, which have been chosen as a model organism for symbiosis and nitrogen fixation studies by many international groups. Particularly, the whole genome of this bacterium was published in 2001 [1,3,6,12].

To make symbiosis possible, bacteria and plant must exchange nutrients as well as signals. The latter is particularly necessary to suppress plant resistance against infection. It is supposed that ABC-transporters are essential for this exchange of signals and nutrients.

Sinorhizobium meliloti have about 200 different ABC-transporters, which makes it impossible to determine the functions of all ABC-transporters exclusively by experiments, but requires to facilitate experimental work through `in silicio'-predictions of physiological functions.

Some recently published articles [4,7,9] show the possiblity of obtaining predictions of functions from phylogentic analyses [5]: They showed, that the molecular phylogeny of ABC-transporters from other organisms is composed of subtrees each containing exclusively taxa with the same physiological function. Thus, phylogenic analysis can be used, at least for this protein super family, to derive hypotheses about physiological functions of proteins: Supposed one calculates the molecular phylogeny for a dataset of sequences of ABC-transporters, where one knows the physiological functions of a part of these proteins, and one finds a subtree in the phylogeny containing exclusively ABC-transporters with the same known function or with unknown function, then one can use as a hypothesis that all the taxa of this subtree have the same function. In this sense, molecular phylogeny can be considered as a generalization of functional predictions by homology searches.

A necessary prerequisite for this way of proceeding is the determination of stable phylogenies, that means: Removing a sequence from the dataset and computing the molecular phylogeny for this new dataset must lead to a tree with the same same topology as the subtree of the original phylogeny with the same taxa.

Some of the ABC-transporters show repetitive structures. As we can demonstrate, this repetitivity together with the necessity of using heuristic methods in algorithms for multiple sequence alignments [8,13] causes instabilities of the molecular phylogenies. To obtain stable phylogenies we must separate the ABC-transporters with repeats suitably in N- and C-termini.

To this aim, a new self-comparison-option of he multiple sequence alignment-program dialign was used. This option excludes the alignment of each site to itself, which makes it possible to use pairwise alignment of a sequence with itself as a search tool for repeats in sequences. Additionally, it yields an appropriate position for the split of an aminoacid-sequence into N- and C-terminus, namely the most right residue of the N-terminus is aligned to the C-terminus of the whole sequence.

After solving these algorithmic problems, one indeed finds a phylogeny with subtrees containing only transporters of a certain substrate and taxa with unknown functions, so that a prediction of the functions of ABC-transporters is possible.

One also obtains interesting information about the evolution of these permeases. In particular, one can observe different duplication events leading to tandem repeats [2] in ABC-transporter or, alternatively, to two ABC-transporters adjacent in the genome of sinorhizobium meliloti. In the second case, one ABC-transporter is more closely related to the N-termini and one ABC-transporter is more closely related to the C-termini of the repetitive ABC-transporters created by the same duplication event. And, they all have the same physiological function.
