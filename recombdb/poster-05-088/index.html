88. Computational Analysis of Gene Products Associated with Autoinflammatory Diseases


Mario Albrecht,1 Francisco S. Domingues,1 Stefan Schreiber,2
Thomas Lengauer1



Keywords: PYPAF1, PYPAF5, NOD2, autoinflammatory disease, structural modeling

Abstract

Variations in the homologous gene products PYPAF1 (CIAS1) and NOD2 (CARD15) have been associated with several autoinflammatory diseases that, although clinically different, share a similar inflammatory pathophysiology [7]. PYPAF1 has been linked to Muckle-Wells syndrome, familial cold autoinflammatory syndrome (familial urticaria), and chronic infantile neurological cutaneous and articular syndrome (CINCA). NOD2 has been found to confer susceptibility to Blau syndrome and one of the two main types of chronic inflammatory bowel disease, Crohn's disease.

The comparative analysis of the genetic variations with respect to their structural impact on the protein level could give important insights into disease mechanisms. Experimental data suggest that the human protein family including PYPAF1 and NOD2 is involved in the regulation of apoptosis and inflammatory immune responses [4,5,6].

We assembled multiple sequence alignments of the family members sharing a homologous domain architecture that contains N-terminal PYRIN (as in the protein pyrin) or CARD (caspase activating recruitment) domains, one intermediate NACHT NTPase domain, and one C-terminal LRR (leucine-rich repeat) domain. The sequence alignments demonstrate that some of the missense variants are located in highly conserved regions of the NTPase domain and possibly impair NTP-hydrolysis.

Furthermore, we could identify suitable structural templates in order to generate 3D models of the different domains, mapping the known variations onto the structure containing specific binding sites. Thus we also provide model structures for the NTPase-LRR domains of plant disease resistance (R) gene products, which recognize pathogen-associated molecules in analogy to mammalian Toll-Like receptors as part of the similar innate immune system of plants, animals, and man [1,2,8,9].

Apart from that, our comprehensive computational analysis revealed the as yet unknown mouse orthologs of PYPAF1 and NOD2 and other human members of their protein family such as NOD1. Surprisingly, this discovery associated the family member PYPAF5 with two distinct functional roles [3], as regulator of inflammatory processes and as hormone receptor.
