21. Combinatorial aspects of the Simplified Partial Digest Problem.

Jacek Bazewicz 1 Marcin Jaroszewski 2 Michael Waterman 3



Keywords: DNA restriction maps, Double Digest Problem, Partial Digest Problem

Introduction.

A creation of a physical map is one of the basic steps in genome studies. Such a map of a DNA strand contains information about locations of markers, which are short and specific sequences of nucleotides. There are many ways of a physical map construction. One of them consists in a digestion of DNA with restriction enzymes. These enzymes cut DNA molecules in the places a short and specific pattern of nucleotides, called restriction site, occurs. What is more, for any given input strand, the number of sites recognized and cleft by an enzyme depends on the time span of a digestion [3], [6].

One possible approach involves two enzymes and reduces to the Double Digest Problem (DDP). This problem is known to be computationally hard. Moreover, a number of distinct, feasible solutions is exponential [2]. Thus, other approaches, based on one enzyme cuts have been proposed. The first, presented in [4] takes as the input lengths of DNA fragments obtained by cutting a target DNA in one, two and all the places the restriction site occurs, a number of the cuts being dependent on the duration of the digestion process. This biochemical experiment reduces then to the Partial Digest Problem (PDP). Its complexity is an open question, but a number of alternative solutions may be bounded from above by a polynomial in a number of restriction sites [4]. Presented algorithms can handle ideal case as well as a limited error data one [5].

In the paper the PDP approach is simplified and the copies of a target strand are split into two sets and two digestions are performed only: a short digestion and a long digestion (thus, the name the Simplified Partial Digest Problem - SPDP). The goal of a short digestion is to have almost all molecules from the first set cut at most once. This can be assured by a properly chosen time span of the reaction. Molecules from the other set are cut in all the places the restriction site occurs due to the long reaction time span (a long digestion). Then, the lengths of obtained fragments are measured during a gel electrophoresis process and recorded. In the paper, a new algorithm for the Simplified Partial Digest Problem is introduced. It compares favorably with the classical version of the PDP approach [5] and with the first SPDP algorithm presented in [1]. A number of distinct, feasible solutions is estimated as well.

Results.

Firstly, to solve the SPDP problem the algorithm has been designed for ideal as well as erroneous data case [1]. As a model of errors the one assumed in [5] had been adopted. The results were quite promising, but still much work had left to be done. In the following paper we presented a new, very efficient algorithm for solving SPDP. The algorithm is based on the matrix of distances between restricion sites and uses neighbourhood rules that are valid for restriction sites in order to construct a feasible solution to the problem. The results of the tests indicate that the algorithm is much faster than the ones presented in [1], [5] and that the number of generated solutions tends to be lower. On the other hand, it is proved that, in the worst case, a number of alternative solutions is exponential. However, the worst case is very rare and may occur only if the data (cuts) are very symmetric.
