89.  Molecular Determinants involved in Odorant
Receptor 5.24 (OR 5.24) agonist activity

Hugues-Olivier Bertrand[1],  Percy Luu[2],  John Ngai2, Francine Acher[3]

Keywords: olfactory receptors, metabotropic glutamate receptors, family3 G-protein coupled receptor, protein-ligand interaction, homology modeling, molecular docking

1.    Introduction

The primary point of contact between the nervous system and the olfactory environment occurs at the level of the olfactory receptor. A structural understanding of the interactions governing a receptor’s selectivity for ligand would provide great insight into the process of olfactory perception. Olfactory receptors belong to the largest family of G-protein coupled receptors (GPCR). In the aquatic vertebrates, the particularity is that olfactory systems are tuned to detect water-soluble stimuli as amino acids. Molecular cloning has revealed that these odorant receptors belong to the G-coupled receptor family 3 which includes the metabotropic glutamate receptors (mGluRs), the calcium sensing receptors (CaSR), the GABAb receptors, vomeronasal V2R receptors and some taste receptors (T1R) [1]. Members of this receptor family are characterized by their long N-terminal extracellular domain (NTD) where the ligand  binding site is found. Extensive information on receptor structure and ligand-receptor interactions is available for the metabotropic glutamate receptors belonging to that family [2,3]. However such data had not yet been described between amino acids and olfactory receptors. In the present study, we have generated a 3D-homology model of the NTD of  OR5.24:  a goldfish olfactory receptor which had been cloned and expressed, and for which complete ligand specificity has been assessed [4]. This receptor is preferentially tuned to respond to basic amino acids such as arginine or lysine, although the tuning is rather broad. Molecular docking  of the 20 natural amino acids was performed . Models were validated through site directed mutagenesis. and functional analysis with calcium imaging. Results are discussed in term of structure-activity relationship and a correlation between binding affinities and scoring function is presented.

2.    Homology model and  Agonist Docking

The NTD of  G-protein coupled receptor  belonging to family 3 adopts a bilobate structure that closes upon ligand binding [1]. The closed conformation is recognized to be the active form of the receptor. A homology model of the OR 5.24 binding domain in its closed form was generated – using MODELER 6.00 (Accelrys, San Diego) - on the basis of the mGluR1 crystal structure (PDB code 1ewk:A) [2] as described for other mGluR subtypes [3].

We have previously shown that a common binding motif can be found in family 3 GPCR that bind amino acids as mGluRs, calcium sensing receptors (CaSR), taste receptors (T1R1, T1R3) and odorant receptor OR5.24 [3]. This motif is composed of five residues that bind the common glycine moiety of amino acids while selectivity is found around the side chain and the distal function (Fig.1). Thus, arginine, the most potent agonist, was bound to these residues in the OR5.24 NTD model. A molecular dynamics revealed several residues that bind selectively the distal guanidinium function. All putative binding residues were mutated and functional assays displayed a loss of potency between 5 to 1000 fold.

Fig.1: Five residues constitute the common binding motif of all amino acid ligands of family 3 GPCR.

The 19 other natural amino acids were then docked using a virtual screening technique: LigandFit (Cerius2, Accelrys, San Diego). For each ligand, a pose was selected according to its binding mode. Poses were subsequently scored using several scoring functions LIGSCORE, PLP, PMF, JAIN as implemented in Cerius2 LigandFit. The best correlation between measured and predicted binding affinities [4] was obtained with the  JAIN scoring fuction.

3.      Conclusion

In the present study we have generated a structural model of the OR5.24 binding site and proposed a general model for recognition of amino acids. Proximal residues coordinate the common amino and carboxylic acid groups, while residues of the distal pocket ensure selectivity. This structural analysis offers an initial view into which receptor functionalities determine the ligand selectivities responsible for odorant discrimination and perception.
