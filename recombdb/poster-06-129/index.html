129. Design of a clinical microarray chip


Jochen Jäger1 and Rainer Spang



Keywords: microarray, medical diagnostics, chip design, clinical trials, sequential methods

Introduction

For microarray based diagnosis only a small number of genes is really substantial. In a preprocessing step the majority of the spots can be filtered out and not used any more. If it were possible to identify the informative spots early in a study, one could save a lot of effort and expenses. We study the problem of how many samples should be analyzed before moving from a full genome chip approach to a smaller and therefore more cost efficient custom diagnostic chip.

Microarray Studies

Currently most clinical microarray studies use the following design approach. Since only few disease genes are known and the study should be as comprehensive as possible the microarray covers as much of the genome as possible. After the initial study is complete the data is analyzed and the most discriminative genes are respotted on a new custom chip that is more cost efficient and can be used for further diagnostic analysis. For studies where the data accumulates steadily over a longer period of time it makes sense to monitor the results as they occur and shrink the chip or even terminate the study either because the discriminative power is already sufficient or the goal cannot be achieved. In the statistical literature this is known as sequential methods [3]. (or group sequential methods [1] if the data is grouped in blocks and analyzed at certain intervals). The problem addressed here is not concerend with variable selection as it is known from the classification literature. Although on first glance we also select a subset of genes, we do this in order to cover as many of the potential candidates as necessary for a consistent and reliable future class prediction. The genes selected do not necessarily have to be the most discriminant ones, they should rather represent a reliable subset on which further feature selection can be applied to. So chip design does not yet select genes for diagnostic signatures, but limits further feature selection to a subset of genes to choose from.

Methods

In order to study the effects of a new chip design during a clinical trial we used a large dataset from leukemia patients (acute lymphoblastic leukemia) [2] and simulated chip design with this dataset. Assume that the final study provides chips for a specific disease variant and chips of another. The goal is to identify a subset of genes that can form a discriminative classifier between these two variants. We would like to compare this classifier with one that only had a fraction of the final chips or not all genes to choose from. Therefore we randomly chose () chips several times. From these chips we extracted the genes that are most discriminative and used these to redesign a new virtual chip. This can be seen as a subset projection of the whole geneset on the original chip. This projection was then applied to all chips so that we had now new "virtual" diagnostic chips. For these diagnostic chips we applied feature selection to extract the most discriminative genes and built a classifier. The performance of this classifier gives a hint on the performance of the new chip design. During the simulation was varied from 2 to and different feature selection schemes were probed.

First results

Figure 1: Classification performance of a simulated diagnostic chip with varying number of "full" genome chips before we switched to the diagnostic chip design

The box plots show the distribution of 20 repeats of randomly picking chips from each group and building a diagnostic chip based on this information. On this leukemia dataset we can see that the classification performance reaches a plateau when using four or more samples for our chip design. The chip was designed using only 100 out of 12600 genes and the classifier was trained using an additional feature selection scheme that chose the best 33 discriminative genes from the 100.
