134. Screening for gain or loss of co-regulation
in cancer specific expression profiles


Dennis Kostka 1 and Rainer Spang 1



Keywords: gene expression profiling, cancer subtypes

Introduction

Microarray experiments can be used to search for and identify subgroups of patients with the same heterogeneous disease [1,2,3]. In the case of pediatric acute lymphoblastic leukemia (ALL) chemo-therapy is adapted to a patient's risk of relapse, and therefore risk-groups have been identified on a cytogenetic basis. It has been shown that it is possible to find these groups on the basis of gene expression data [3], which is freely available to the public.

In our work we use this data to look for groups of correlated genes which lose their coherence in a specific risk-group. In this way we find genes that are not necessarily differentially expressed, but which take part in a regulatory scheme corrupted in that group. Specific genes exhibiting this kind of behavior can therefore be characteristic for a risk-group.

Method

To find groups of genes showing the above mentioned expression pattern, we make use of a test statistic defined in the following way: The samples of the experiment are divided into two groups: One group (A), in which we look for co-regulated genes that lose their correlation in the second group or risk-group (B).

The searching for the co-regulated genes is implemented as follows: We calculate the correlation matrix of the 1000 most variable genes (using only the samples in group A) and scan column-wise for genes with a correlation coefficient greater than 1-. The parameter determines how well the genes should be correlated to that one specific gene determining the current row of the correlation matrix. The genes we find by this method form a group of genes (G), which is dependent on the column of the correlation matrix and the parameter . Having selected these genes by their correlation to one specific gene, we next construct a measure of the correlation of all of the genes in G among each other: We project their expression values (using only the samples in A) onto the first principal component. The fraction of variance conserved by this procedure () then yields a quantity we can employ: if it is close to one all the genes in G are tightly correlated, whereas smaller values stand for increasing decoherence.

Searching for genes which lose co-regulation in group B, we also calculate their projection onto their first principal component using the samples in B. If the fraction of variance conserved during this process is called , the final test statistic reads: . The subscript G denotes that the statistic is dependent on the group of correlated genes, and therefore on the column of the correlation matrix as well as the choice of the parameter . Maximizing means looking for genes which lose co-regulation in group B most prominently. To look for genes which gain co-regulation in B, one simply has to swap the groups A and B.

We maximize the test statistic by choosing the appropriate column in the correlation matrix, and therefore get a ordering of the clusters we found. To test the statistical significance of these genes, we sample from the null hypothesis by repeatedly permuting the samples, which enables us to calculate a corresponding -value. First tests indicate, that it is indeed possible to find significant clusters of genes for different risk-groups.
