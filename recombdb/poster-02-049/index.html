49. Using Hidden Markov Models and Support Vector Machines to Determine the G-Protein specificity of a given G-Protein Coupled Receptor (GPCR)

Orkun S. Soyer[1], Bin Qian[2], Richard R. Neubig[3], Richard A. Goldstein[4]

Keywords: Hidden Markov Models, Support Vector Machines, classification, G-Protein coupled receptors, G-Proteins, evolution, sequence analysis.

1     Introduction

G-Protein Coupled Receptors (GPCRs) make up one of the largest protein superfamilies. Found in a range of species from yeast to mammals, these membrane embedded proteins facilitate communication between the cell and its environment. This essential function makes them a key figure in many diseases, including Alzheimer’s, anxiety, depression, and Parkinson’s.

As the name of the superfamily suggests, GPCRs function through the intermediary of a heterotrimeric guanine nucleotide-binding protein (G-Protein). Upon binding a signaling entity (ligand) on its outer surface, the receptor activates the G-Protein, which dissociates into its subunits, allowing them to react with downstream effectors. G-proteins constitute another large protein family that are divided into four subfamilies based on amino acid similarity of their a subunits; Gi/o, Gq/11, Gs, and G12. The difference between members of each subfamily lies in the identity of downstream effectors they interact with. Acting on such different effectors each family of G-Protein allows the receptors to initiate diverse set of physiological responses. Thus it is necessary to find the identity of the G-Protein a receptor couples to, in order to understand its role on cell physiology.

Here we evaluate the application of three different computational methods in determining the G-Protein specificity of a given receptor, using its primary sequence. First we use two methods from the alignment dependent approach based on Hidden Markov Models. Second we use an alignment independent method coupled with Support Vector Machines. Used with intracellular segments of receptor sequences, both approaches achieve high levels of success in determining the G-Protein specificity of receptors. The results show that the alignment dependent HMM methods are robust towards the composition of training and test sets, proving their capacity to provide a more reliable model for G-Protein specificity. The SVM based method is less successful in this task, mainly due to problems associated with choosing alignment independent features correlated with G-Protein specificity.

2     Software and files

In order to represent an alignment dependent approach we used two HMM based methods; HMMer [1], and T-HMM, an HMM that incorporates phylogenetic relations among proteins into model creation process [2]. For an alignment independent method we make use of the general idea of transforming protein sequences into features, independent of their length. Such transformation allows comparison of proteins without aligning their sequences. The transformed sequences could then be analyzed and classified using general statistical tools such as principal component analysis. Here we use an SVM algorithm to carry the classification of derived features.

This analysis is constrained on receptors from aminergic and peptide subfamilies, which make up the most studied group of GPCRs. The sequences are derived from March 2002 release of GPCRdb, a GPCR specific database [3]. Those sequences for which there is no coupling information, or dual coupling information reported in the latest TiPS Nomenclature Supplement are eliminated. This procedure resulted in 470 sequences, which are labeled according to their G-Protein coupling specificity.  In order to find the intracellular segments of these sequences we used THMM, a web-based HMM for predicting the location of transmembrane helices in membrane proteins. We eliminated receptors that are predicted to contain more or less than 7 transmembrane helices by this program, resulting in 418 sequences for the analysis. The final set of sequences are labeled according to their G-Protein specificity and partitioned in two, to be used as test and training sets in a “two-fold-cross-validation” format.

Grouping receptors in training set according to their G-Protein specificity, we used their alignments to create a model with T-HMM and HMMer. A test sequence is then scored against each model and the highest score determined its G-Protein specificity. For the SVM based application we converted both training and test sequences into sequence-extracted features. These were the lagged cross correlation among three amino acid z-scales, the average of the three z-scales over the sequence, and the length of the intracellular segment used. Once every sequence is transformed into these features an SVM model is trained on the training set and used to classify the test sequences. The SVM classification is carried using SVMTorch, publicly available SVM software [4].

3     Figures and tables

We carried the above-explained analysis, using different partition techniques on the original data set. “Even-Odd Split” results in homogeneous distribution of subfamily members among training and test sets, while the “Random Split” could result in a heterogeneous distribution. Finally the “Subset Exclusion” method specifically excludes certain subfamilies from the training set, to be used as test set. These different partitioning procedures resulted in increasingly difficult training and test cases, challenging the performance of each method.
