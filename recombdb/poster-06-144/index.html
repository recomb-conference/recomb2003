144. Selecting genes by Bayesian Inference


 Volker Roth 1



Keywords: Gene expression, sample classification, automatic relevance determination.

Introduction

Microarray experiments typically measure the expression levels of several thousands of genes simultaneously. In the context of cancer diagnostics, the discrimination between either diseased and healthy tissues, or between different kinds of diseased tissues, constitutes a major application for this technology. The aim is to improve the understanding of cancer pathogenesis on a molecular level. A central goal of the analysis of microarray data is the identification of small subsets of informative genes with cancer-specific expression profiles. From a machine learning viewpoint, this problem of identifying relevant genes is known as the problem of feature selection in supervised learning. For microarray experiments with several thousands of genes, however, the combinatorial explosion of the space of all possible subsets of features precludes the use of greedy search strategies. This paper reports on the successful application of a Bayesian inference method to the problem of selecting discriminative subsets of features. Contrary to most other approaches in the literature, our method implements a wrapper strategy for gene selection by directly optimizing the discriminative power of a logistic classification model. The selection process can be interpreted as a special instance of the Bayesian automatic relevance determination (ARD) principle. For technical details see [1]. The most outstanding properties of this combined classification and selection method are: (i) excellent generalization ability; (ii) probabilistic outputs, rather than only binary class labels; (iii) generic definition of a doubt class collecting samples with uncertain predicted label; (iv) simultaneous assessment of prediction strength and stability of gene selection under resampling; (v) availability of a highly efficient optimization algorithm which is capable of dealing with large-scale real-world applications. Note that the latter is particularly important for wrapper methods.

Experiments for several microarray datasets demonstrate both the outstanding classification performance and the biological relevance of the selected genes. All these features make it possible to use our GenLASSO software2 in a ``black-box'' manner for high-quality analysis of large-scale microarray datasets.

Results

We applied our method to several microarray datasets, e.g. the ones published by Golub et al, [2], and by Alon et al, [3]. In all experiments, the Generalized LASSO classifier was able to separate the classes with a cross-validation-error that compares favorably to previous studies. Due to the high standard deviations, however, we feel that their is no evidence for preferring one method over the other ones solely based on estimated error rates. The real advantages of our method over most related approaches are the following: the possibility of quantifying the classification confidence allows us to naturally introduce a doubt-class that collects samples with uncertain class membership. In the left panel of figure 1 the empirical distribution of classification confidences both for the correctly classified samples and for the misclassified ones are depicted (Leukemia dataset). It it obvious that correct classification correlates with high confidence, whereas misclassifications are widely spread over the whole range between 0.5 and 1. In the right panel, the influence of introducing a doubt-class by thresholding is depicted. By leaving 20% of the samples unclassified, we obtain a classifier with vanishingly small expected error for the remaining 80% of the samples. For practical applications in cancer diagnostics, such a doubt class is certainly an appropriate statistical model: taking into account all possible experimental uncertainties and noise sources involved in a microarray experiment (see eg. [4]), one usually is willing to leave a certain fraction of samples with uncertain labels unclassified.
Figure: dataset. Left: empirical distribution of classification confidence levels both for correctly classified samples (black) and for misclassified samples (grey). Right: effect of thresholding by confidence level. Error rate (solid) and fraction of samples in doubt class (dotted).


A second advantageous property is the gene selection mechanism being independent of the actual test-sets during resampling. For a fixed classification model, we can simultaneously estimate the error rate (without selection bias!) and the stability of the selected genes under different splits of the dataset. The expression patterns of the highest-scoring genes (figure 2) clearly indicate that they are significantly over-expressed in one of the Leukemia classes. A careful analysis of the high-scoring genes shows that all automatically identified genes are highly relevant for discriminating the classes from a biological viewpoint.
