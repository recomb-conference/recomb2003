66. Discriminative Learning of Composite Regulatory Modules


Tomer Naveh, Tommy Kaplan, and Nir Friedman 1



Keywords: Discriminative Learning, Composite Binding Sites, Regulatory Modules.

A central goal of molecular biology is to uncover transcription regulation circuits that govern gene expression. These circuits involve transcription factors that act in concert to modulate the transcription of genes. The recent advances in high-throughput assays, such as microarray experiments and ChIP localization, allow us to infer groups of genes that are co-expressed and co-regulated. The challenge is to use this wealth of information to gain insight about transcriptional regulation. Several works have confronted this challenge. Most of them focus on finding overabundant motifs within the regulatory regions of the co-expressed group [1,3]. As these works show, one can often discover binding sites that have also been verified experimentally. This general approach, however, might fail as those promoter regions often contain inherent genomic phenomena that are not specific to the genes under study.

In this work, we present a procedure for locating regulatory modules in promoter regions. A module represents the binding sites of a pair of transcription factors that act in cooperation. Our approach is discriminative, searching for modules that are overabundant in the promoter regions of the target group of co-expressed genes and are infrequent in the control group of genes outside the target group. By doing this, we filter out phenomena that are shared among both groups, leaving us with the core motifs.

Our method works as follows. We consider 2 groups of promoter sequences. The first is the group of promoters of co-expressed genes (typically a few dozens) and the second is all other promoters (typically a few thousands). We characterize a module as a pair of PSSMs each representing the binding site of one transcription factor, requiring both sites to occur in proximity to each other. We evaluate such modules with respect to the promoter groups by using a discriminative likelihood function that extends the approach of [4]. Maximizing this function results in a module that is expected to be found with high probability in promoters of the co-expressed group and with a much lower probability in all other promoters. We find parameters that maximize the likelihood function using a conjugate gradient ascent method, ensuring convergence to a local optimum.

We evaluate our method on synthetic data and yeast promoters. To test our method, we created a synthetic dataset matching ``real world'' parameters. The set consisted of 5070 sequences of length 500. In 50 of those sequences we planted a pair of motifs, generated from known yeast PSSMs [5]. The motifs were planted in various distances up to 50 bps apart. These were labelled as ``co-expressed'' together with other 20 random sequences, acting as false positive noise. The other 5000 sequences were used as the control group. We then applied our algorithm, and reconstructed the planted module (see figure 1a). We used the learned module to classify a similar test set. For each sequence, we computed the posterior probability of being regulated by the module. Figure 1b shows the cumulative percentage of genes for which the posterior probability exceeds the threshold.

Figure 1: (a) Motifs generated from top PSSMs were planted in 50 of 5070 promoter sequences of length 500. Another 20 sequences were used as a false positives. The bottom module was learned, correctly reconstructing both motifs. (b) Evaluation of learned module on 5050 unlabelled test sequences. Using posterior probability of 0.85 or higher, will accept 77% of the regulated sequences, with about 1% of false positives.

Next, we used our method to analyze groups of genes from ChIP localization assays in Yeast. Based on the results of Lee et al [2], we constructed several groups of putative combinatorial co-regulated genes. Each group is supposedly co-regulated by (at least) two transcription factors, and contained a few dozens of genes. The rest of the genes (about 6000) were used as a control group. We trained on two thirds of the data, and tested the learned module on the remaining promoters. Figure 2 shows the performance of our algorithm on test data from one of these runs.
