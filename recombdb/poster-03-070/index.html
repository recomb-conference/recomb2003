70. Genomic Comparisons Between and Within Species and their Use to detect Alternative Splicing

Martin Stephan[1], Jürgen Kleffe1 and Thomas Wiehe[2]

The phenomenon of alternative splicing is one of the reasons that the total number of genes of an organism is not neccesarily related to its complexity. An extreme example is the Drosophila dscam gene, where alternative splicing may lead to more than 38,000 gene products from a single gene. For about 49 of these products experimental validation has been obtained [1]. Clearly, in such cases it is generally difficult to decide how many and which products are realized in nature. Simple EST searches would be of little help, since the majority of possible mRNAs may escape detection and would therefore not be represented in EST databases. Alternative splicing in the Drosophila dscam is mediated by clusters of mutually exclusive exons. This special form of alternative splicing poses an additional problem for automatic gene finding programs. Since many programs use linear exon chaining to maximize some gene assembly score, the typical - and wrong - outcome in cases of genes with mutually exclusive exons would be the one with the maximum number of exons. We show that genomic comparisons within and between species may come to help to circumvent the above problems. It appears that mutually exclusive exons are often due to several events of micro-duplication in the evolutionary history of a species. We developed a tool to pre-process a genomic sequence in order to detect such self-similar regions and to tag potential mutually exclusive exons. We systematically study the evolution of this form of alternative splicing in an across-species-comparison of genes which interact with neurotrophic factors.

[1] Schmucker, D., Clemens, J.C., Shu, H., Worby, C.A., Xiao, J., Muda, M., Dixon, J.E. and Zipursky S.L. (2000). Drosophila Dscam Is an Axon Guidance Receptor Exhibiting Extraordinary Molecular Diversity. Cell 101, 671-684.
