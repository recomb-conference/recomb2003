104. Computational Mutagenesis Using an Empirical Four-Body Potential

Zhibin Lu and Iosif Vaisman1

Keywords: protein structure, mutations, Delaunay tessellation

Nonlocal interactions between the amino acid residues play important role in the specificity and stability of protein structures. Methods of computational geometry, including Delaunay tessellation, provide a robust and effective approach for quantitative description of nonlocal residue contacts in proteins [1,2]. An empirical four-body potential derived using Delaunay tessellation of structures from a representative protein data set helps facilitate understanding and interpretation of structure-sequence and structure-function correlations in proteins.

A training set of 1,000 non-homologous high quality X-ray structures of protein chains without gaps in sequence was selected from PDB. These proteins were tessellated using single point per residue (Ca) representation. Distribution of log-likelihood values for all residue combinations in the Delaunay quadruplets was used as a four-body statistical potential. This knowledge-based potential has been used to study the effects of mutation on protein activity and stability. Each residue in a protein participates in a number of Delaunay quadruplets. Total score for a residue is calculated as a sum of log-likelihood scores of all Delaunay quadruplets in which this residue participates. This score for all residues along the sequence is shown in the upper plot in Fig. 1. To evaluate the effect of mutation we simply change the identity of one or more residues in the protein and recalculate the score. Then we subtract the mutant profile from that of wild type protein and get a difference profile shown in the lower plots of Fig. 1 for D185E, D185V, and D185Y mutants of HIV reverse transcriptase. The difference profiles reflect the magnitude of change that is caused by residue replacement and identify all residues affected by such a change.

Correlation between the changes in calculated protein scores caused by mutations, experimental activity and thermodynamic data, is shown in Fig. 2. Here the average change in potential for all replacements in a set of almost 400 mutants of HIV reverse transcriptase [3] is correlated with the experimentally determined activity of mutants. For mutants with the higher than a wild type activity the average change in potential is positive, for less active mutants the negative change in potential is proportional to the decrease in activity. A strong correlation suggests significant predictive power of the computational mutagenesis method.
