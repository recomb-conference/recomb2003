16.  Amino acid composition of genomes, lifestyles of organisms and evolutionary trends: a large-scale analysis

Fredj Tekaia[1], Edouard Yeramian[2] and Bernard Dujon1

 

Key words : hyperthermophiles, mesophiles, thermostability,  correspondence analyses.

1 Introduction

Global understanding of lifestyle properties characterizing living organisms through relevant genomic signatures is a major aim of genome projects [1,2]. Considering amino acid composition in 55 predicted proteomes (See Figure 1) a large-scale analysis has been performed using correspondence analysis [4]. The obtained distribution allows to gain better understanding of the relative importance for the various components involved in the intricate relations connecting amino acid compositions with other biologically important features including lifestyle and evolutionary trends.

 

2 Materials and methods

A matrix T of 55 species and 20 amino-acids was constructed (with Tij defined as the number of occurences of the amino-acid j in the species i, divided by the total number of amino acids in i and multiplied by 100). Three dummy variables and eleven observations were added by considering corresponding average amino-acid compositions. The dummy variables are: ‘char’ for charged amino acids, ‘pol’ for polar/uncharged amino acids  and ‘hyd’ for hydrophobic amino-acids. The dummy observations are: MES, TH, HTH denoting respectively mesophilic, thermophilic and the hyperthermophilic species; and  SPC, E, A, B, EA, EB, AB and EAB denoting respectively genes specific to each species, genes conserved exclusively in one domain of life [E : in eukaryal species, A : in archaeal species and B : in bacterial species]; genes conserved in a combinaison of 2 domains [EA : eukaryal and archaeal,  EB : eukaryal and bacterial and AB : archaeal and bacterial] and in the intersection of the three domains [EAB : eukaryal, archaeal and bacterial].  These sets of genes are obtained by large-scale proteome comparisons as described in [5,6]. Correspondence analysis is then performed on the obtained 66 lines and  23 columns matrix (see http://wwwalt.pasteur.fr/~tekaia/genomesaa.html ).

3 Results (see Figure 1)

1)Correspondence analysis of the amino acid compositions allows to discriminate between the three known lifestyles (mesophily/thermophily/hyperthermophily) ; 2) For various organisms, amino-acid composition properties are essentially driven by GC content, and to a significantly lesser extent by growth temperatures associated with lifestyles. The respective contributions of these two components are 61% and 17%. These proportions are essentially unchanged with respect to a previous analysis [7], which involved only 15 genomes, available at the time; 3) In terms of amino acid compositional biases, two specific ‘signatures’ for thermophily (in a broad sense, including hyperthermophily) can be detected. First, thermophilic species display a relative abundance in glutamic acid, concomitantly with the depletion in glutamine. Second, in thermophilic species, the relative abundance in glutamic acid (negative charge) is significantly correlated (r=0.83 with p<0.0001), with the increase in the lumped ‘pool’ Lysine+Arginine (positive charges). This correlation (absent in mesophiles) could be interpreted on physico-chemical bases, relevant to the thermostability of proteins;  4) A statistically significant difference is observed between the average lengths of the genes in the surveyed species, following their distribution between the three domains of life. Also a significant difference is observed between the average lengths of themophilic (283.0 + 5.8) versus mesophilic (340 + 9.4) genes. It is thus possible that the general shortening of the primary sequences in thermophilic proteins plays a role in thermostability ; 5) Considering various combinations of conservation properties (genes conserved exclusively in Eukaryotes, in Archaea, in Bacteria, in combinations of two and three domains) correspondence analysis reveals a trend towards thermophilic-hyperthermophilic profiles for the most conserved subset of genes (ancient genes) ; 6) When limited to the subset of “species-specific” genes, correspondence analysis leads to a different picture for the clustering of genomes following amino-acid compositions (not shown): for example,  the ‘core’ specific part of a genome can bear lifestyle signatures different from those of the complete genome.

7) Detailed material and results are in [8] and in in : http://www-alt.pasteur.fr/~tekaia/sacso.html
