137. Kernel based supervised learning for cancer phenotype prediction and gene selection

Eisaku Maeda 1, Tomonori Izumitani , Ichiro Takemasa 2,
Kenichi Matsubara 3, Morito Monden


Keywords: gene expression analysis, support vector machine, kernel method, recursive feature elimination




Advanced informatics such as supervised learning and feature selection technique provides a powerful tool for predicting cancer classes and identifying marker genes, which are critically important for disease diagnosis and treatment[1, 4]. DNA profiling data are captured from a few thousand genes, and then each sample is characterized as a vector in a `gene space', whose dimension is equal to the number of genes. By contrast, the number of samples is usually much smaller than the gene space dimension. In this type of case, traditional techniques such as Fisher's discriminant function and the k-nearest neighbor method (KNN) do not work well. Support vector machines (SVM) generally achieve better performance, particularly under the above condition, and have been successfully applied to various classification problems including gene expression analysis [4]. Another technique, the kernel based nonlinear subspace method (KNS), which is a extended subspace method using kernel PCA, also exhibits high performance under the above condition[3], and has not yet been tried for gene expression analysis.

We applied these two kernel based techniques, SVM and KNS, to two kinds of gene expression analysis, histopathological phenotype prediction in colorectal carcinoma [5] and multi-class cancer diagnosis[4]. From among an initial set of 71 samples (56 well and 15 poorly differentiated), 23 samples were randomly sub-sampled as a test set; the remaining samples were used as a training set. Error rates were estimated using all 2313 genes. This process was repeated 100 times. As shown in table 1, KNS performed better than SVM on the phenotype prediction. On the multi-class cancer classification, the accuracy was almost same as that provided by SVM. Figure 1 shows the prediction accueracy with smaller sets of genes, which was estimated by combining classifiers KNN, SVM or KNS with a gene selection method, recursive feature elimination[2]. The figure reveals KNS performs better than SVM and KNN a wide range of selected-gene number. These results suggest kernel based supervised learning methods can be powerful means for gene expression analysis.
