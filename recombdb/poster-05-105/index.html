105. Rules generator for prediction of
protein secondary structures

Piotr Łukasiak[1], Peter L. Hammer[2], Jacek Błażewicz[3],

Keywords: protein prediction, machine learning, secondary structure prediction, LAD

1     Introduction

Protein sequences are encoded in DNA, the holder of the genetic information. In principle, it should be possible to translate a gene (DNA) sequence into an amino acid sequence, and to predict the three-dimensional structure of the resulting chain from this amino acid sequence. The different physical and chemical properties of the side-chains determine both the local and global conformations adopted by polypeptide chains. While peptides do not always exhibit ordered three-dimensional structure, most proteins consistently fold into the same configuration. The goal of the analysis presented here is to create a system which allows to receive as the output the protein secondary structure, based on its primary structure being an input. Another goal is to find rules responsible for this effect. As compared with others, the approach presented gives satisfactory results (PHD [4][5], MODLEM [6]).

2     Method

The Logical Analysis of Data (LAD) [1] method was used to solve the above problem.

Figure 1: Modified Logical Analysis of Data method’s stages and phases.


Secondary structures were reduced into three main classes: α-helix (H), ß-strand (E) and the rest (X). Making a transformation from a protein sequence to the set of observations a concept of windows [3] of length equal to 7 was used. A window of length 7 generates an observation with 7 attributes (a-3, a-2, a-1, a0, a+1, a+2, a+3) representing a secondary structure corresponding to the amino acid located in place a0. During experiment 54 physical and chemical properties of the amino acids’ residues offered by ProtScale have been taken into account. Examples were obtained from the Dictionary of Secondary Structures of Proteins (DSSP) [2]. Originally LAD approach is designed for two classes, so one had to introduce some modifications (c.f. Figure 1), which allowed to classify into three classes in the classification scheme.

3     Experiments and results

During experiments 85 protein chains have been chosen into consideration (20 000 observations have been created]). The first part of experiments has been made for each property separately, it means that at a time, each observation consisted of 7 attributes – each of them was derived from the same property. Average accuracy for the best property for classes H and E were over 55%. During the second part of experiments observation consisted of 14 attributes, where the first 7 corresponded to one property, and the last seven corresponded to another property. Average accuracies for class H and E was over 75%. Rules obtained during experiments are easy to interpret by domain experts (c.f. Table 1).

4  Conclusions

In the context of machine learning algorithms modified LAD gave results similar to the best standing alone methods used for protein prediction problem. In the molecular biology context LAD passed the boundary of 70% of accuracy prediction. It has been shown that not only certain global features, such as the presence of helix or strand structure can be predicted with a usable accuracy and reliability using the LAD method, but also rules of the secondary structure creation were constructed.
