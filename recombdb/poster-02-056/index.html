56. AnCoRe: Analysis of Complementary Residues

Urs Wiedemann[1],  Dirk Labudde[2],  Gunnar Kleinau[3],  Gerd Krause[4]

Keywords: interaction prediction, complementary residues, amino acid properties

1     Introduction

Most cellular processes depend largely on specific inter- and intra-protein interactions. However, classical experimental analyses of these interactions are unfortunately expensive and time consuming. To reduce the number of wet lab experiments necessary, good working hypotheses are needed. Such working hypotheses can in many cases nowadays be obtained by deciphering the hidden information within the vast amount of sequence information available. Especially alignments of homologous proteins sharing a common fold contain information of great value, which are utilized in the approach presented here.

The specificity of protein-protein interactions is mediated for the most part by the properties of the amino acid side chains. Only complementary properties, suitable for the interaction between amino acid side chains, will allow for binding of the respective poly-peptide chains. For the interaction to remain throughout the course of evolution, a change in the property of one of the interacting partners has to be compensated by a corresponding change of the other partner. These complementary mutations can be spotted in multiple-sequence alignments and permit the identification of potentially interacting residues.

Correlated mutation analysis (CMA) was established by Goebel et al. in 1994 [2] as one method to identify such potentially interacting residues and has already been successfully applied to many different problems, exemplified by Horn et al. [3]. In CMA two residue positions of a multiple sequence alignment are evaluated for the similarity of the amino acid change pattern. Goebel et al. found a relationship between the correlation coefficient of two residue positions and the chance that the two residues are in contact with each other. However, the correlation coefficient does not directly take the properties of the respective amino acids into account. Here we propose a different approach which specifically factors in the amino acid property complementarity.

2     Objectives

AnCoRe is used to analyze multiple-sequence alignments of homologous proteins, which share a common fold, for potentially interacting residues. To find these residue positions, the complementarity of the amino acids is evaluated by comparing the properties of the participating amino acids. AnCoRe can be used to predict residue interactions within one protein (single multiple-sequence alignment), as well as interacting residues for protein-protein interactions sharing a conserved binding mechanism (two corresponding multiple-sequence alignments, each for one of the interacting partners).

3     Principle

The complementarity of two residue positions in one multiple-sequence alignment (intra-protein) or two corresponding multiple-sequence alignments (inter-protein) is evaluated by AnCoRe using three scores:

1.            CIS – the amino acid class interaction score: Amino acids are grouped into classes corresponding to similar amino acid properties. The complementarity on the amino acid class level of two residue positions is then quantified by CIS. CIS is the mean score of all class pairs at the two residue positions.

In addition to predefined class definitions [4], the user can supply customized ones. The class interaction matrix [1], defining the corresponding interaction scores, is also user customizable. This enables the user to search for specific interaction types only (e.g. charged interactions, aromatic interactions, etc.) while suppressing unwanted less specific potential interactions.

2.            AIS – the amino acid interaction score: AIS is the mean score of all amino acid pairs at two residue positions taken from an amino acid interaction matrix [1]. The amino acid interaction matrix is also user supplied, to enable the use of different matrices. By additionally calculating the AIS the coarse grained results from evaluating the CIS can be refined while retaining its capability to filter out less likely interactions.

3.            CBS – the class break similarity score: The class breaks pattern is obtained by evaluating the changes of amino acid classes from sequence to sequence at a given homologous residue position. The comparison of two patterns for two residue positions for similarity yields the CBS. The CBS emphasizes the simultaneity of property changes at the two residue positions.

The three scores are combined into a composite RIR score, which is used to rank the potentially interacting residue pairs. Additionally it is possible to analyze each score individually to focus only on a specific aspect.

4     Applications

AnCoRe is a fast and easy to use tool which aids the user in analyzing multiple-sequence alignments for potentially interacting residues if no other prior knowledge is available. It can be applied to intra-protein interactions for evaluation of the protein fold, especially in homology modeling applications. It can also be applied to inter-protein interactions, by analyzing two corresponding sequence alignments, to identify residues potentially responsible for the interaction of the two respective sets of proteins.
