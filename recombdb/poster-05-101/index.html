101. Improving resolution and accuracy in hydrogen-exchange mass spectrometry


Neil C. Jones, 1 Bryant Forsgren, 2 Pavel A. Pevzner 3



Keywords: isotopic exchange, hydrogen exchange mass spectrometry

Abstract

Amide hydrogens along a protein's peptide backbone exchange with hydrogen, or its isotopes, from a solvent. The rate of exchange of any particular amide hydrogen is governed primarily by the accessibility of solvent to that hydrogen [1]. Knowing the exchange rate of each amide hydrogen in the protein's peptide backbone yields constraints as to which hydrogens are solvent accessible, and hence, to the protein's basic conformation. Both NMR [2] and Mass Spectrometry (MS) [3] are used to measure isotopic exchange rates. In hydrogen exchange mass spectrometry, the protein is isotopically labelled for a controlled amount of time, and then broken into smaller peptide fragments, each of which is analyzed independently by MS.

Current experimental techniques for determining isotopic exchange rates through mass spectrometry yield only rough estimates of exchange rates over large portions of a protein, typically lumping hydrogens into one of three rate classes (fast, medium, or slow). We demonstrate that it is both computationally and experimentally feasible to extract more accurate and more precise exchange rates for single amino acids by collecting data in a slightly different way, and analyzing that data with a more natural mathematical model. In particular, by breaking a protein into overlapping peptide fragments, one can apply linear programming and nonlinear regression to calculate individual amide hydrogen exchange rate constants. By fitting the chemical kinetic equation for each amide to all the observed data, finer resolution and higher accuracy are achieved.

We present a straightforward computational algorithm to determine exchange rate constants from HX MS data. We compare our algorithm to the current data analysis methods from both MS and NMR HX experiments, and describe the factors that influence prediction error and how these are affected by experimental error.
