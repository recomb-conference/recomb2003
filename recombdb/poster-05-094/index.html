94. Multiple Structural Alignment by Order-Independent Secondary Structures


 Oranit Dror 1, Hadar Benyamini 2, Ruth Nussinov2 3, Haim J. Wolfson1



Keywords: Structural Bioinformatics, Partial alignment, Subset alignment, Non-sequential alignment, Non-topological motif, Supersecondary structural motif.

Abstract

We present a novel highly efficient algorithm for aligning multiple protein structures and detecting common structural motifs. The rapidly growing number of protein structures increases the need of advanced structural alignment tools. Indeed, many algorithms for pairwise structural alignment have been developed. However, only a few methods are currently available for addressing the multiple case. Multiple structural alignment is a much more powerful tool for protein classification, structural motif detection, evolutionary research, and structure prediction. Most of the existing methods for multiple structure alignment are based on a series of pairwise comparisons. Our method, MASS (Multiple Alignment by Secondary Structures), is a truly multiple-based application: it considers all the given structures simultaneously. Additionally, it is able to detect structural motifs common not only to the whole input set of molecules, but also motifs common only to its subsets. Exploiting the secondary structure information aids in filtering out noisy solutions and achieves efficiency and robustness. The method disregards the sequence order of the secondary structure elements and thus is capable of detecting non-topological structural motifs. MASS is suitable for a broad range of challenging applications: It can handle large-scale protein ensembles (on the order of tens) that may be heterogeneous, noisy, topologically unrelated and contain low-resolution structures.


Software availability: http://www.cs.tau.ac.il/ornit/MASS.

Method Strategy

MASS is based on a two-level alignment, using both secondary structure and atomic representation. In the first stage the method represents the proteins by their secondary structure elements (SSEs). Each SSE (a helix or a strand) is represented by its axis, which is defined by the least squares line from all it atoms. Then, Geometric Hashing [1] is used to efficiently detect pairs of SSEs, whose spatial configuration appear in several (or all) given proteins. Such local SSE similarities define initial multiple alignments between the respective proteins whose common cores contain at least two SSEs. In the second stage the initial multiple alignments are extended into global ones by clustering and detection of spatially conserved residues, which do not necessarily belong to SSEs.

Results

The following examples demonstrate the power of MASS in addressing various interesting cases of structural alignment (see http://www.cs.tau.ac.il/ornit/MASS for details).


Non-topological motif detection: MASS was applied to an ensemble of 10 all- proteins belonging to 4 different folds and 6 different superfamilies. It detected a conserved bundle of 4 -helices (Figure 1a). Figure 1b shows that the alignment is non-topological i.e. the order and the direction of the corresponding SSEs along the polypeptide chain are not conserved.

Subset alignment detection: We applied MASS to an ensemble of eleven proteins belonging to five different families of the 'PLP-dependent transferases' superfamily. All eleven proteins were aligned with a core containing the complete seven-stranded -sheet of the fold and three -helices. Moreover, a subset alignment of ten proteins has been detected with an additional -helix. This subset alignment agrees with a previously suggested evolutionary relationship tree that has been generated by all-against-all pairwise comparisons.


Large-scale structural alignments: We applied MASS to several structural families that contain between 66 to 80 molecules. The running times were between 1min:28sec to 1h:25min on a standard PC (Pentium© 4, 1800 MHz, 1GB RAM). They were influenced by: (i) the number of molecules; (ii) the average molecular size (and the average number of SSEs in a molecule); and (iii) the structural variance among the molecules.

Conclusions

MASS successfully handles a range of cases that are considered hard for multiple structural alignments. These include ensembles of large number of molecules (order of tens), non-topologically related molecules and different sub-types of molecules. MASS is likely to be useful for analysis of proteins where only secondary structure information is available, like theoretic models and cryo-EM density maps.
