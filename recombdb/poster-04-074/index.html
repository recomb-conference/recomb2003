74. Three Dimensional Functional Model Proteins: Structure, Function and Evolution

Benjamin P. Blackburne[1], Jonathan D. Hirst[2]

Keywords: Diamond Lattice, Minimalist Models, Evolutionary Landscape, HP Model Protein

1 Lattice Models

Protein evolution proceeds through natural selection based on function. Function is determined by the three dimensional structure of the protein. Many proteins contain a binding pocket or active site, and this is an obvious focus for examining fitness. In order to consider molecular evolution on a broad scale, we need to consider vast evolutionary landscapes. To do this we employ a coarse-grained or lattice protein model using the shifted-HP folding code[1]. Residues are reduced to a single point on a lattice, and assigned as either hydrophobic or polar. This allows us to exhaustively search conformational space and sequence space, and find sequences that fold to a thermodynamically stable ground state. If a ground state structure has a binding pocket, it is said to be functional. We measure fitness by the number of hydrophobes in the binding pocket, an aspect relevant to non-specific hydrophobic binding.

Figure 1: An example of a 23-mer diamond lattice protein. Dark residues are hydrophobic, light residues are polar. A ligand is shown to illustrate the position of the binding pocket.

Previously [2] [3], we have considered functional model proteins on the two-dimensional square lattice. Here we consider the three-dimensional diamond lattice. An example of a functional model protein is shown in Figure 1.

2 Evolutionary Landscapes

By an exhaustive search of sequence space, we are able to generate a fitness landscape, where we consider sequences connected by point mutations drawn as a graph (figure 2). We note several features of our landscapes. Each landscape is made up of one or more neutral networks, connected by adaptive mutations. A striking feature is the presence of “hub” sequences, which are able to make all these connections individually and which connect to the other hubs. These sequences, shown around the centre of figure 2, will allow rapid exploration of the different neutral networks.

Another feature is the presence of evolutionary bottlenecks, where there is a restricted path from one area of the landscape to another. One significance of this could be in the prevention of drug resistance. If evolution to drug resistance proceeds through such a bottleneck, mutants along the path could be specifically targeted, reducing the likelihood that drug resistance would evolve.

Figure 2: 86 member family of 25-mer functional model proteins. Each node corresponds to a sequence with fitness represented by shape (circle=3 hydrophobes, triangle=4, square=5, pentagon=6, hexagon=7) . Edges represent a single point mutation.
