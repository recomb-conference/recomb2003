83. Stochastic Mechanisms of Evolution:
The Number of Domains-to-Protein Links Predicts the Number of Genes in Genomes

Vladimir Kuznetsov[1], Valery Pickalov[2]

Keywords: proteome complexity, evolution, number of genes, stochastic processes, Kolmogorov-Waring distribution

1     Introduction

Recently, we proposed a novel statistically-justifiable approach for estimating the number of  protein-coding genes in partially-sequenced and partially-annotated genomes  [1-4]. Using different data sets, we have estimated that there are 32,200-42,700 genes in the human genome [3-5]. Our gene number prediction methodology employs information about the number of evolutionary conserved protein-coding sequences (protein domains/families/motifs) distributed in proteins within a sample proteome from large number of fully sequenced genomes. 

In this particular work, we developed the evolution analysis that consists of revisiting the protein-coding gene count for human, mouse and Arabidopsis thaliana.

2     Methods and Software

        We have analyzed the statistical distributions of the number of the non-redundant domain-to-protein links of the InterPro domains (protein domains, families, motifs [http://www.ebi.ac.uk/interpro/ ; updated on Dec 16, 2002]) in proteins of the sample proteomes of the 90 fully sequenced genome organisms of three kingdoms of life: Archaea, Bacteria and Eukaryota [http://www.ebi.ac.uk/proteome/ProteomeSource.html/; updated on Dec 17, 2002]

3     Results and Discussion

We found that many protein domains that appear in nature are widely distributed in phylogenetically distinct species and progressively appear in new proteins due to evolution. A large fraction of the identified distinct InterPro domains (i.e. unique domains, homologous domain families, motifs) in these 90 proteomes (1409 (23%) out of 6085 domains) are found in at least one organism in each of these kingdoms of life. The 1666 (49%) out of 3427 human protein domains are found in at least one of the archaeal or bacterial organism. We found similar numbers of domains shared with Archaea and Bacteria for mouse and the A. thaliana domain sets. We counted the number of non-redundant occurrences of the given distinct domain in all proteins of the organisms. By non-redundant, we mean that a distinct domain is counted only once even if it occurs several times in the same multi-domain protein. We referred to this number as the number of  non-redundant domain-to-protein links of a distinct domain in the proteome of the organism.

All the observed domain-to-protein link frequency distributions for these 90 proteomes are universally well fitted by a family of skewed Pareto-like probability functions (the Waring, Simon-Yule, Champernowne Zipf distributions). These probability functions are relatively robust to experimental errors. The family of these functions is associated with the probabilistic model of progressive domains evolution which is a steady state distribution of a linear birth-death Markov random process described by the forward Kolmogorov equations [5]. Note, this evolution model does not explicitly include the selection processes. However, the birth and death parameters of the model could be driven by the selection forces. Our statistical analysis of the distributions associated with steady-state solution of the model implies certain simple scale-invariant rules of progressive evolution for the number of protein domains and their occurrences in proteins within the proteomes. In particular, we found strong linear relationship between the number of protein-coding genes, G, and the total number of non-redundant domain-to-protein links in a sample proteome, M (r=0.998). This relationship was fitted by the regression model G=0.8506M for the 87 proteomes (excluding mouse, A. thaliana and human) and through the use of the extrapolations this model predicts 33560, 25910, and 26870 protein-coding genes in human, mouse and A. thaliana proteome, respectively. The standard errors for these estimates are 60, 50 and 50 domains, respectively.  We also found strong linear relationship between the number of protein-coding genes, G, the number of non-redundant domain-to-protein links for mostly redundant domain in a proteome,  the total number of non-redundant domain-to-protein links in subsets of the 100 or 300 most widely occurring protein domains found in  studied fully-sequenced genomes. Correlation coefficients are 0.93, 0.993 and 0.996, respectively. In the latter two cases, the 33900 and 34720 human genes are predicted by the linear regression models, respectively.  Through counting of the mean value by our three estimates, we predicted that there are 34060 protein-coding genes in the human genome.

4    Conclusions

         Proteomes of the diverse organisms can be specifically related to one another in terms of the distributions of the  number of non-redundant domain-protein links.

         Our whole-genome comparison of domain occurrences in Archaea, Bacteria and Eukaryota proteomes implies even if the size of the genome is large, the number of protein-coding genes in the organism is determined by a small number (300 or less) of the evolutionary conserved and functionally essential sequences (domains) contained within proteins of many studied organism.

         Our statistical modeling and observations suggest a  random component in innovation, birth and death processes for vast majority of evolutionary conserved protein-coding sequences as well as positive selection and  preferential propagation of certain fraction of  already existing conserved protein domains in certain species.
