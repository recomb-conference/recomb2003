45. Optimum Nucleotide Background Frequency Measures for DNA Motif Detection

Krzysztof M. Rajkowski[1].

Keywords: DNA motifs, nucleotide background frequencies

1     Introduction

Reliable detection of DNA motifs, such as transcription factor binding sites, is essential for genome decryption. Motif detection methods, such as Profiles [1,4] or Hidden Markov models [2], ultimately involve comparing what is found in a sequence with what would be expected by chance, for DNA the latter being calculated from nucleotide background frequencies or probabilities. Usually nucleotides are assumed to have a zero order Markov distribution in DNA, at least to a good approximation, but this does not appear to have been tested. The availability of sequenced chromosomes makes an empirical test of this assumption, and a search for more realistic assumptions, possible as described here.

2     Methods and Results

Using the human chromosome 22 sequence as the databank, the observed frequencies of each nucleotide and each di-, tri- ... nona-nucleotide (henceforth n-nucleotide, n = 2 - 9) were determined. The test consisted of comparing the distribution of observed frequencies of each of the 4n n-nucleotides with that of their calculated expected frequencies according to the M0 assumption. Assumptions of first, second and third order Markov (M1,M2,M3) distributions and of a biconditional distribution (B2)(found superior to M0 for amino acid background frequencies in protein motif detection [5]) were also tested. The expected frequencies of a polynucleotide AGTA...(observed with a frequency p(AGTA...)) according to these assumptions would be calculated as:

M0                p(A)p(G)p(T)p(A)....

M3                p(A)(p(AG)|p(A))(p(AGT)|p(AG))(p(AGTA)|p(AGT))...

B2                p(A)(p(AGT)|p(A)p(T))(p(GTA)|p(G)(p(A))...

The Wilcoxon signed rank test for paired samples was used to test whether observed and expected frequency distributions were significantly different by the M0 assumption, though it would be expected to give conservative results as it measures the difference in median values between two distributions and probability distributions have, by definition, identical mean values.The Jensen-Shannon divergence DJS [3], designed for probability distributions, was also used but it varies non-linearly with the difference between two distributions. Empirically for two-valued distributions, however, it can be linearised (r2 > 0.999) such that the degree of identity between two distributions (I) is given by I = 1 - DJS0.473 (I=0 to 1). (I is equivalent to DJS as a measure of which of two assumptions is more realistic). Also, the normalised rms (rms/0.25n) between observed and expected frequencies was used to compare the different assumptions with each other.

                The Wilcoxon test showed that the expected and observed frequency distributions for the zero order Markov assumption were significantly different at the p=0.05 level for n = 5 and at the p=0.01 level for n=6. It can be seen that that the third order Markov assumption, which is used in the Bioprospector programme [2], gives the most realistic background frequencies except for the octa- and nonanucleotides when it was the biconditional assumption. (If the expected third order Markov frequencies were calculated as p(A)(p(AG)|p(A))(p(AGT)|p(A)p(G))(p(AGTA)|p(A)p(G)p(T))... (for the above example) then the biconditional assumption would be most realistic for all values of n). The zero order Markov assumption gave results very similar to those obtained if it is assumed that all n-nucleotides in a set are equiprobable (p=4-n) (not shown). It was not possible to determine if the biconditional assumption was also more realistic for longer n-nucleotides - the databank used was not large enough for adequate sampling of decanucleotides and, indeed, 428 (0.16%) of the nonanucleotides were absent from chromosome 22.

In conclusion, zero order Markov nucleotide background frequencies should be avoided if possible as they are little better than assigning a background frequency p = 4-n to all polynucleotides of length n. Third order Markov background frequencies are the most realistic of those tested overall and the slight superiority of biconditional frequencies for octa- and nonanucleotides is probably not sufficient to warrent using the latter.
