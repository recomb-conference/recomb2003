114. Beta Edge Strands in Protein Structure Prediction and Aggregation.

Jennifer A. Siepen[1] and  David R. Westhead1.

Keywords: secondary structure prediction, machine learning, edge strands.

As a result of the genome and other sequencing projects the prediction of the three-dimensional structure and function of proteins from amino acid sequence is becoming increasingly more important.  The prediction of the secondary structure and the arrangement of the secondary structure elements within the protein are a valuable first step in the elucidation of three-dimensional structure and protein function.

Two distinct tertiary contexts of a beta sheet may be defined, "central strands, bordered on both sides by other beta strands, and edge strands, bordered on only one side by another beta strand" [1].  It is well established that recognition between exposed edge strands of beta sheets is an important mode of protein-protein interaction and can have pathological consequences for instance the aggregation of proteins into a fibrillar structure is associated with a number of predominantly neurodegenerative disorders [2,3].  Despite this there are relatively few cases of aggregation or insolubility in natural beta sheet proteins, and recently Richardson, 2002 identified that charged residues, proline residues and/or beta bulges are commonly found in the edge strands of beta sheets to protect from interactions with other beta strands.  These protective features are unfavorable in the interior strands of the beta sheet and as a result, tend to lower the beta sheet propensity of the edge strand [4].   This and other studies demonstrate characteristic differences and different beta sheet propensities between strands located towards the edge of the beta sheet and those occupying a more central location.  These observations are likely to be reflected in the classic three-state secondary structure prediction accuracy.  Here we show that edge strands are predicted significantly worse with respect to central strands by the secondary structure prediction software PHD [5] and PSI-PRED [6] on a representative set of 564 protein domains. 

The problem of distinguishing edge strands from central strands based on sequence information alone is important in predicting residues and mutations likely to be involved in aggregation, and is also a first step in predicting folding topology.  A decision tree method (c4.5 [7]) and a support vector machine approach (SVMTorch [8]) have been adopted to classify edge strands from central strands in a representative set of protein domains. An initial SVM classification from amino acid sequence of strands assigned based on structure (DSSP [9]), achieved a mean classification accuracy of 78.4%, which is significantly better than expected by chance (50%).  Following this we investigated the accuracy of the method with predicted strands, and the use of the method in improving secondary structure prediction.
