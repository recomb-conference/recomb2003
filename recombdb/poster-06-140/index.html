140. Discriminative Clustering Analysis


Francisco J. Molina 1



Keywords: gene expression, microarray, clustering

Introduction

To analyze microarray expression data, we introduce a clustering algorithm that finds groups of genes with similar expression patterns. The found clusters are ranked according to their capability to discriminate between two or more kinds of samples. Instead of clustering based on only the similarity between expression profiles, the algorithm takes into account a classification of the samples in disjoint categories. This classification is based upon one or more experimental conditions under which the samples were taken. These conditions could be extracelular environments, i.g., ``in vivo'' and ``in vitro'', or different types of breast cancer, i.g., ``sporadic'', ``brac1'' and ``brac2''. By using the label of the microarray samples, we provide extra information to cluster genes.

The algorithm iteratively extracts the most discriminative cluster within the remaining genes. The discriminative capability of a cluster quantifies how much a cluster discriminates between sample classes, that is, how differently the genes in that cluster are expressed across sample classes.

To fix ideas, let us suppose that several microarray experiments are performed and that we are given certain cluster of genes used in those experiments.

To compute the discriminative capability of , we assign a probability density to every sample class and then we quantify the overlapping between those probability densities. The bigger is the overlapping, the less discriminates across sample classes. When this overlapping is large, for a wide range of expression levels, the infered probabilities of observing those expression levels in different sample classes is similar. This makes more difficult to distinguish between those sample classes by considering only the expression data from the genes in cluster .

Once identified a cluster of genes that is very discriminative, we can label a new sample by assigning it to the sample class that maximizes the likelihood of the new sample's expression data.

The more discriminative the cluster is, the smaller is the probability of missclassifying the new sample.
