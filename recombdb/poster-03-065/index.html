65. Cross-species comparison and structural properties of regulating sequence elements involved in alternative splicing

Friedrich Möller[1], Ulrike Göbel[2], Jürgen Kleffe1 and Thomas Wiehe[3]

Splicing is in part regulated by short sequence motifs, the so-called intronic and exonic splice site enhancers and silencers. They are located in exonic and intronic parts of a gene, usually not more than a few nucleotides long and interact with the molecular splicing machinery during the splicing process. Due to their short lengths it is difficult to determine their location on the genomic sequence. They take an important role in the process of alternative splicing and the interaction with different SR-Proteins (Serine-Arginine rich proteins) leads to the effects of alternative splicing such as exon skipping and intron retention, which often also depends on the developmental state or type of cell.

We prepared a dataset of alternatively spliced genes of different species using EST clustering. From this we extracted sets of homologous genes of different species where the cluster pattern indicated that alternative splicing may take place. We examined those sets, looking for relationships between regulatory elements in different species and forms of alternative splicing. For the determination and localization of these enhancer and silencer motifs we considered not only the raw sequence data but also derived structural properties of the putative regulatory elements. We show that a detailed comparison of orthologous regions on the level of DNA-structure can enhance the ability to detect alternatively spliced exons.
