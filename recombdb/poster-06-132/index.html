132. Robustness evaluation of clusterings

Hans A. Kestler[1],[2], Andre Müller1,2, Malte Bucholz2,
Günther Palm1, Thomas M. Gress2

Clustering is an important tool for the generation and evaluation of hypotheses. This is especially true for data generated from microarray and comparative genomic hybridization (CGH) experiments. This data is usually of high dimensionality and low cardinality. The commonly used hierarchical clustering methods are as such applied only in a biased way in that there is no measure of the trustworthiness of a particular result. Here, we will present a measure of robustness of clusterings and give results on two different data sets: (a) CGH data from prostate carcinomas1 and (b) leukemia gene expression data2.

Cluster robustness is assessed through repeating the prototype based cluster procedure with fixed parameters, i.e. number of clusters and training parameters, distance metric, but under varying initial conditions, i.e. initial cluster centers, presentation of data points. A cluster solution is then called robust if the fluctuations in the cluster assignments of the data points among the different runs are small and the individual clusterings are in that manner consistent to each other.

The new measure we introduce is based on the pairwise similarity between set partitions (score) and can be interpreted as the mean proportion of samples being consistent over the different clusterings. The cluster correspondence problem is hereby solved via a linear assignment procedure3. The concept is illustrated in the following diagrams:

In this investigation we applied the proposed robustness evaluation measure to three different prototype based algorithms (self-organizing feature maps (SOM), k-means- and fuzzy c-means clustering) and to data sets from Mattfeldt et al.1 and Golub et al.2. The two following graphs show the averaged robustness score (MCA) over a varying number of cluster centers for the different cluster algorithms and the data sets:


 


To correctly judge the found results the performance of a random cluster algorithm is included as a base-line. This suggests for the prostate carcinoma data 3 clusters and for the leukemia data 2 clusters, as the difference of the MCA values of the fuzzy c-means (leukemia) and the self-organizing feature map (prostate) to the respective random clusterings is maximal in these cases. The two class scenario of the leukemia data is confirmed in the 2 cluster result2. Analysis of the data assigned to the three clusters of the prostate carcinoma cases showed a differentiation into different grades of malignancy1.

The proposed method of assessing cluster robustness is a general method not limited to prototype based cluster algorithms, but also applicable to hierachical methods. In comparing the found robustness results to a random clustering a measure of trustworthiness in the clustering result can be established.
