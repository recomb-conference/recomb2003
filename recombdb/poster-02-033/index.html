33. A new method of finding functional
sequence motifs and its application to human
GC-rich genomic sequences.

Tadashi Imanishi[1], Toshihide Shikanai[2], Shin Nurimoto[3], Taichiro Sugisaki[4]

Keywords: motifs, relative entropy, GC contents

1     Introduction

Aside from gene coding sequences, eukaryotic genomes contain important functional sequences such as regulatory elements of gene expression and chromosomal replication.  However, many of them remain uncharacterized.  Finding functional sequence motifs in genomic sequences is thus a very important and urgent issue in bioinformatics.  Here, we developed a new method of finding sequence motifs, with the aim of discovering unknown functional sequence motifs in eukaryotic genomes, and we implemented the method in a computer program called OSMO-finder (Over-represented Sequence MOtif finder).  In this paper, we summarize the outline of the new method and describe some results of its application to human genomic sequences.

2     Method

The purpose of the new method is to find over-represented sequence motifs without gaps in a set of unaligned sequences.  For this purpose, we first prepare "seeds" of motifs by listing all sequence segments of length W in the data set.  We then search for sequence matches to the seeds, and calculate the profiles and consensus motifs based on matched sequences.  Finally, we count the number of occurrences L of a specific sequence motif of length W in the data set, and calculate statistical measures for assessing the validity of the motifs identified.  Two types of statistical measures are used.  One is the exact probability (P) of finding the motifs L or more times in random sequences with the same size as the data set, while the other is L times D, the relative entropy based on profiles of motifs[1].  We developed a computer program for this method, and named it OSMO-finder.

3     Results and Discussion

We applied OSMO-finder to find motifs in GC-rich DNA sequences from human chromosome 2.  After masking repetitive sequences by RepeatMasker[2], we chose the 10

most GC-rich DNA segments of 10kb from human chromosome 2 (NCBI build 30), and searched for motifs of 10 bps.  We could successfully identify various kinds of sequence motifs that are abundant in the data set.  Top ten motifs with smallest P values had extremely high values of D x L (Table 1), suggesting that these measures are both useful.  Some of these motifs were identified also by MEME[3].  Interestingly, these motifs overlap only partially with known sequence motifs in TransFac[4].  Therefore, the motifs identified by OSMO-finder could be novel functional motifs in eukaryotic genomes.  We believe that our program could be a powerful tool for identifying hidden sequence motifs in genomes of various species.
