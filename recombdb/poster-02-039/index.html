39.  Large-Scale Clustering of Proteins with Sequence Similarity Based on the Extraction of Maximal Cliques

Sarah Mohseni-Zadeh, Pierre Brézellec, Jean-Loup Risler

Keywords: protein clustering, clustering algorithm, maximal clique, multidomain proteins, sequence similarity

1     Introduction

The wealth of recent data from sequencing and structural annotation of genomes has led to a rapid enrichment of structural and functional identification of predicted protein sequences. While some proteins yet has been experimentally characterised, most of them were annotated on the basis of shared sequence similarities and 30 to 60% (depending on organisms) have unknown functions. Massive intra- and inter-species sequence comparisons are preliminary steps helping a better understanding of sequenced proteomes [3]. However, grouping the sequences into families or clusters allows a more rapid interpretation of the huge amount of data and brings new insights into mechanisms of action and interaction of proteins. Most clustering databases and softwares are dedicated to a further type of biological research such as the identification of orthologous proteins or the collect of protein motifs. Besides, the great majority of existing methods has established proteins connections on the basis of single transitive closures. These approaches resulted in the case of big sets of sequences, in a massive chain effect due to multidomain proteins.

2        An algorithm for large-scale classification of proteins

We designed a general and rapid classification method for protein sequences, suitable to the analysis of limited sequence samples as well as for the clustering of many compared proteomes. This method does not intend to distinguish between paralogs and orthologs. Our clustering principle is aimed to be simple. Hence, only one selected criterion of connectivity chosen by biologists is used at all the steps for comparing elements. We tended to combine three principal requirements:

1/ In each cluster, the level of connections between all the elements is close to the maximum

2/ Chain effects must be avoided but to some extent, transitive linkages are not excluded because they may suggest interesting information on gene fusion events.  As a consequence, all the sequences in a given cluster may not share the same domains while the proximity of two motifs in a small number of elements gives clues for the evolutionary study of the whole group.

3/ We are aware that characterisation of proteins cannot solely rely upon sequence homology. Nevertheless, it has been widely proved that all-by-all massive sequence comparison may be an effective approach for many predictions of biochemical functions or shared properties. It is therefore essential that each multidomain protein ought to be attributed to all the clusters where its different putative functions are represented. We allow these multiple representations.

The algorithm for classification was devised into three essential parts:

1/ In order to keep the highest level of connectivity we developed an alternative method for the search of maximal cliques. Indeed, an exhaustive detection of maximal cliques requires an excessive computational complexity and proved to be unapproachable for very connected sets of thousands elements. Instead, we chose to search a limited but large number of cliques. From each sequence, we constituted a group of all its connected elements (Z>14).

Any group that verifies a maximal clique properties (a- within the maximal clique each element has pairwise links with all the others, b- No external element to that clique could verify the rule a-) is then considered as a core cluster.

2/ Each core cluster grows with new elements that possess at least one connection (Z>14) with any sequence of the core cluster. This step increases the chance of classifying multidomain proteins into more than one cluster.

3/ Lastly, the clusters that share more than 80% elements were iteratively combined to form a bigger common cluster. These groupings last as long as it remains a new cluster sharing enough elements with any subcluster of the preceding iteration. Experimentally, we could show that the biggest clusters that contained redundant information were unified and became easier to further biological analysis. 

The algorithm was implemented in Perl and C++.  It is fast and robust.

3     Applications

Our first application was the classification of 48811 plants sequences extracted from Swissprot and TrEMBL [*]. These proteins could not be correctly clusterized with a single-transitive linkage method since a mass chain effect grouped half of them together. The first step calculation of alignment scores and Z-scores were executed by a software package[1]. It included the pairwise comparisons of all sequences and additional runs with randomizations of each sequence. In that case, our criterion for connectivity was a Z-score >14, given that a significant similarity has been admitted for Z-score >10 [2]. They generated 2.4 106 pairwise links involving 43626 proteins and leaving 5185 singletons.

This example yielded 3917 clusters versus 2809 previously obtained by a transitive closure method. The largest cluster contained 2180 sequences mainly related to kinase proteins. Similar computations could be performed for a group of 143000 sequences from 53 bacteria. Reliable assignment of proteins to their correct functional groups was proved. A biological analysis could be partially done for the distribution of Arabidopsis thaliana sequences. By comparison, each of the Arabidopsis protein families listed in the web server "The Arabidopsis Information Resource" (www.arabidopsis.org), showed one or more corresponding specific groups in our clustering. This was for instance true for ABC-transporters, kinases, ion channels, cyt. p450 and heat shock proteins.

These results show that we have designed and developed a fast algorithm for general classification of proteins, applicable to a large number of proteomes. It is much more sensitive than the methods using transitive linkages. Indeed, the level of total connections is equal or higher in all the clusters since they derive from maximal cliques. In addition, mass chain effect is always suppressed. Lastly this sort of clustering represents multidomain proteins with the possibility to include them in several clusters. As a result, we better reflect their functional assignment and correlation with other proteins. The biological relevance of the algorithm has been proved by comparing the clusters composition to known protein families. We have began to apply this method for the massive clusterization of 80 compared proteomes (including archea, bacteria, yeasts, plants, Caenorhabditis elegans, Drosophila melanogaster and mammalian species) in the context of Teraprot project (http://www.infobiogen.fr/services/Teraprot/).
